## Terra, the Hope of Peace

> [!quote|mark] Book of Wynne, Verse XXXII
> _“And the Elder Gods rested, their hands withdrawn from the shaping of stone and star.  
> The world turned in their absence,  
> And for a season, all seemed as it should be.”_  

When Nosduh and Ogul stepped away from Terra, the air itself seemed to exhale. The Ideal Gods took up their stations without question at first, guiding their peoples, shaping laws, and raising temples of impossible grandeur. The seas hummed with trade, the skies glittered with dragons’ wings, and the land was dotted with shining cities. It was an age spoken of in poetry and song as _The Gentle Reign_.

The first cities rose as marvels of divine inspiration. **Vel’thamar**, capital of the Elves, was carved into the living trunks of colossal moonwood trees, each building a spiral of white-silver grown, not built. **Stoneforge**, home of the Dwarves and Loxodon, was a city within the roots of a mountain, its streets lit by rivers of molten gold that never cooled. Across the waves, **Pearlspire** floated upon a ring of coral, the jewel of the Tritons, while inland the **Sunplain City of Oraven** hosted markets where Humans, Genasi, and Leonin bartered side by side. These names are spoken now as myths, for none survived the shattering to come.

Cassierrel, the first dragon, circled high above all these realms, her shadow a comfort and a warning. She was not a god — not yet — but her presence was as close to divine as mortal eyes could behold. Born of the Elder Gods’ will, she was tasked with guarding the balance they intended for Terra. From her, in four mighty clutches, came twenty-one dragons, each a living storm, glacier, inferno, or gale. Though they were not yet worshiped, mortals whispered prayers when their shadows passed overhead.

For centuries, the harmony seemed unbreakable. The Ideal Gods met in councils beneath the Worldtree, where they spoke of ways to strengthen their children’s prosperity. Temples gleamed. Crops flourished. Mortal craft reached heights unseen even in the Forgotten Realms. The great aqueducts of Locathah stone still haunt the ocean floor today, relics of that age.

But harmony is delicate, and even the smallest fracture in the divine can echo through creation.

The seeds of dissent sprouted in whispers — first among the mortals, then among the gods themselves. The Ideal Gods, though young in divinity, were not blind. They remembered the day the Elder Gods withdrew. They remembered the silence. To be left without the hands that made you — it gnawed at them. Some took it as abandonment. Others as a challenge to surpass their creators.

> [!quote|mark]  Book of Wynne, Verse XXXVII
> _“The child who is left alone in the garden will either tend the flowers or burn them.”_ 

Among the mortals, philosophies bloomed in dangerous shapes. The Truth-bound Tabaxi began to speak of a Terra where no god’s hand steered fate. Minotaurs of Temp gathered in great plains, training not for war but for the day they might stand against their makers. Even the Genasi of Eaos, who prized order, began to write scrolls questioning the necessity of divine command.

Cassierrel watched all of this with wings spread wide, issuing decrees where she could, sending her children to quell disputes before they became wars. She reminded the Ideal Gods of the Elder vision, warning them that the world could crack if they used it as an arena for their rivalries. They listened… at first.

But rivalry is a tide that rises without sound. As the centuries rolled on, the Ideal Gods began to carve Terra into spheres of influence, not merely for stewardship but for prestige. Glory’s cities began to rival Luck’s ports. Revenge’s shadow wars undermined Truth’s priesthoods. Logic and Rage could not speak without drawing their swords.

It was in the City of Oraven that the first defiance of divine law was committed openly. A coalition of Humans, Orcs, and Yuan-Ti declared themselves _Unbound_, swearing allegiance to no god. They razed the temple district, sending offerings meant for the gods into the river in a single night of fire. Cassierrel herself descended, her roar splitting stone, but even her presence could not undo the idea that had taken root: _that mortals might shape their own fate without divine will._


> [!quote|mark] Book of Wynne, Verse XLI
> _“Once the seed is sown, the tree will grow — whether the planter wills it or not.”_  

From their distant watch, Nosduh and Ogul saw the order they had labored for begin to unravel. The brothers, still estranged, did not rush back. Each wanted to see whether the other’s children would falter first. But as rebellion spilled from city to city, as dragons were forced to take sides, and as the Ideal Gods began to sharpen their quarrels into blades, the Elder Gods knew the season of peace was ending.

Cassierrel’s last plea to the Ideal Gods came beneath the boughs of the Worldtree. She warned them that pride and division would invite destruction beyond any of their imagining. But her words fell like rain on stone. Within a century, her prophecy would be fulfilled — and her own sacrifice would change the heavens forever.
