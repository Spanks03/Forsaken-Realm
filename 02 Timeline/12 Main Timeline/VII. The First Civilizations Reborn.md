### Out of Ashes, Into Stone and Song

The silence that followed the Cataclysm was not empty. It was filled with the weeping of survivors, the cracking of scorched earth, and the sigh of wind over bones that once lived. For a generation, Terra felt more grave than world. Yet even in the bleakness, the stubborn spark of life endured. Crops were planted in ruined valleys, fires were kindled in broken hearths, and slowly, the idea of tomorrow began to return.

It was the **dwarves** who first carved permanence into the land. Driven by instinct and memory of their old ways, they sought the safety of mountains. They burrowed into the jagged range that came to be called the **Spine**, chiseling their first post-Cataclysm strongholds. There, in stone halls still blackened by the starfall, they rediscovered ore and flame. At first their forges were crude, producing little more than iron nails and brittle spearheads. Yet within a century, steel rang once more in the dark, and dwarves began to call themselves a people reborn. he first steel reforged after the Cataclysm was celebrated not as a weapon, but as a miracle. The dwarves preserved much knowledge of the old world in stone, carving histories into cavern walls so they would never again be burned away.

> [!quote|mark]  Fragment from the journal of Drenn Coalhand <br> F. R. 112 Pre Rail
> _"We found not shelter, but promise, in the Spine. The mountain keeps secrets, but it also remembers. If we listen long enough, the stones will teach us how to endure."_  

The **elves**, by contrast, sought not steel but beauty. They wandered forests that were only stumps, rivers that had turned to mud, and began the slow task of coaxing them back to life. Their first cities were not fortresses but sanctuaries of song, woven from light and memory. They painted their grief in murals on broken cliffs, binding sorrow into art so that others might endure it. Elves became archivists of the soul, protectors of story in an age when words were as fragile as seedlings.

The **humans** rose more hesitantly. They had neither the long memory of elves nor the stubborn roots of dwarves, but they carried resilience. Settlements of mud and timber sprouted along rivers, fragile and small. Mortality taught them urgency, and within a generation, their numbers grew. Where dwarves remembered and elves mourned, humans adapted. They spread quickly, filling gaps left by other peoples, carrying sparks of innovation in their restlessness.

Beneath the waves, the **tritons** rebuilt their halls. In the drowned ruins of the cataclysm, they discovered relics older than memory, glowing obelisks and currents carved by divine wrath. With discipline and pride, they reestablished order in the seas, declaring themselves keepers of balance against chaos. Their society bore the echo of Atlantean grandeur — proud towers of coral and stone, where philosophy flowed as freely as tide.

> [!quote|mark]  Oral account from the tribe of the Ashen Plain
> _"My grandfather told me he walked for ten years before he found a place to stay. He said the land itself was restless, like a wounded beast. Maybe that is why we, too, could not stand still."_  

Not all survival was noble. In the shadow of ruin, famine and desperation drove some to raiding, cannibalism, and tyranny. There are records of entire valleys ruled by warlords who claimed divine sanction, demanding blood and fire as offerings. Yet even in these places, seeds of change grew. Slaves who remembered old prayers whispered them to children, and broken tools became relics carried into better days. Civilization does not rise in perfection — it rises in struggle.

The first **trade routes** were not roads but rivers. Small flotillas of dugout boats began to connect settlements, carrying grain, hides, and salt. From these exchanges came the fragile beginnings of diplomacy. A dwarven miner might trade with a human hunter, or an elf might sing the history of a river to goliaths who had only just named it. From such small acts, trust — and rivalry — began to grow.

Even language bore scars of the Cataclysm. Common tongues fractured, regional dialects thickened, and in some places entirely new languages emerged. Dwarves deep in the Spine developed words that echoed the rumble of stone. Elves lengthened their vowels to mimic the wind through trees. Humans mixed fragments of both into a rough speech that carried far, if not elegantly. In the absence of divine order, mortals stitched their own Babel.

> [!quote|mark]  Testimony recorded by Scholar Ysena of Thurn <br> F. R. 189 Pre Rail
>_"We speak three ways in my village: the tongue of my mother, the tongue of my father, and the tongue of my neighbors. None of them are the same, yet somehow we understand."_  

By the end of the first century, fire no longer meant fear but hearth. Fields spread where once there had been craters, and songs rose where once there had been silence. The world was still wounded, but Terra was no longer a grave. It was a cradle. Mortals had begun not only to survive, but to _remember_.

And remembering, they began to dream again.

### Of Gods, Dragons, and the Fractured Weave

The silence of the Elder and Ideal Gods hung heavy after the Cataclysm. Shrines rose again in the ashes, and prayers were whispered into the still air, but only rarely did an answer come. The world’s wounds were too raw, its scars too fresh. Mortals, accustomed to gods who had once walked beside them, now struggled with faith that felt one-sided.

It was in this silence that the **Lesser Gods** began to stir. They were not born from mortal ascension, nor were they fragments of older deities. They were divine beings in their own right—children of the Ideal Gods or shaped of their essence, yet never meant to rule as high as the Elders. Where Nosduh and Ogul had withdrawn into slumber, and the Ideal Six had been broken or bound, the Ideal Twins, **Yearn** and **Datarr**, and Lesser Gods walked carefully among mortals.

They were present, but measured. When worship was offered, they often answered—not with overwhelming miracles, but with subtle shifts, signs, and whispers of guidance. Their restraint was deliberate, for the memory of the Cataclysm weighed on them as much as mortals. They had seen too much interference unravel the balance. Now they held themselves back, vowing never to ignite such ruin again.

> [!quote|mark]  Elarion <br> Wandering Chronicler of the First Century After the Fall
> _“They no longer shake the heavens with every word. Instead, the gods leave coins on the table of a beggar, or a flame burning where none should burn. I fear I prefer them this way.”_  

Among the most beloved of these divine figures were the **dragons**, the children of Cassierrel. Her sacrifice had etched itself into the very bones of Terra, and her offspring bore the weight of her legacy. No longer merely beasts of sky and flame, they had become **Keepers**—embodiments of truths and principles that shaped mortal life.

There was the Keeper of the Hunt, whose eyes pierced the wilderness with predator’s clarity. There was the Keeper of Hospitality, who watched hearths and halls, ensuring that the ancient customs of welcome and shelter endured. There was the Keeper of Friendship, whose unseen wingbeats guided bonds between people, and the Keeper of Ambush, who hid in shadow and storm, teaching the wary to never grow complacent. Each dragon-Keeper embodied a different truth, and mortals began to honor them not merely as rulers of the skies, but as **gods who governed the patterns of mortal existence itself**.

> [!quote|mark]  Inscription on the walls of the rebuilt city of Telharrow
> _“A stranger under my roof is sacred. For in them, the Keeper of Hospitality may be testing my heart.”_ 

The worship of the Keepers spread quickly, especially among cultures that sought clear guidance. To hunt was sacred; to welcome a stranger was divine; to betray was dangerous, for the Keeper of Ambush watched. In these teachings, mortals found comfort and structure. The Keepers did not overreach, as the Ideal Gods once had. They lived their truths, and mortals followed.

Yet even as divine order began to steady itself, **mortal invention surged**. New races emerged, not from the will of gods, but from the resilience and desperation of survivors. The **Dragonborn** were born when dragons—whether Keepers or lesser kin—took mortal form and mingled their bloodlines with humankind. The **Half-Elves** came from bonds between humans and elves, bridging cultures that had once stood apart. The **Halflings**, smaller and hardy, were said to have come from dwarves and humans. The **Half-Orcs** rose from violence and union alike between human and orc tribes.

Other creations were deliberate, and darker. Some mortals, yearning to replicate divine craftsmanship, began experiments that blurred the line between man and beast. From these attempts came **Shifters**, whose blood carried echoes of wolves, bears, or hawks, and **Simic Hybrids**, whose forms twisted under arcane grafting. Magic itself was bent to forge life—**Warforged**, beings of steel and spellwork, their souls bound into bodies that should not breathe, yet did. Ancient Harmonians in fear of extinction, took fate in their own hands and made the **Harmonians** as they fade into the shadows of history.

> [!quote|mark]  Lirros of Thanehold <br> Dwarven Artificer of the Genesis Forge
> _“The Warforged are proof of our triumph, and our folly. We bound souls as though we were gods. But tell me—where do those souls go when their shells rust?”_  

The gods did not craft these races. They came of mortal hands and mortal will. Some saw this as hubris. Others saw it as proof that mortals no longer needed gods to shape their destiny.

But magic was no longer a steady river. Since the Cataclysm, the **Weave lay torn**, its currents pulled too far and too often until the threads thinned and snapped, and in those wounds, the Void seeped through. Mortals who tapped too deeply into its power often found themselves burned by it. Healing spells, blessings of strength, enchantments of swiftness—all became dangerously addictive. Some whispered of men who begged to be wounded just so they could feel the flood of a cure spell once more. At first, these ripples were unnoticed, but soon sages began to see the truth: each spell stretched the **Soul Fibers**, the unseen strands that bound mortal spirit to mortal flesh.

The people called this condition **Arcane Radiance**—a soft glow of reliance that grew brighter the more one leaned upon magic. Radiance made the body crave further spells, as though life itself demanded arcane reinforcement. But with each indulgence, the Soul Fibers grew weaker, unable to return fully to their natural state.

Inevitably, those who bore too much Radiance suffered **Arcane Trauma**. This was no simple exhaustion, but tearing of the soul itself: memory fractured, the body weakened, the spirit left scarred. Some never recovered. Others endured only by time, rest, or the rare touch of a new substance, which could mend the stretched fibers. Even spells of restoration risked worsening the problem, for they too added to Radiance.

In the centuries that followed, priests and scholars wrote grim warnings:

> [!quote|mark]  Fragment of the Radiant Codex <br> F. R. 212 After Silence
> *“Beware the hand that heals too often, for in its mercy may lie your undoing. To live by magic is to die by magic, unless the soul is tempered by suffering.”* 

Thus, where once mortals thought of magic as pure gift, now they treated it with wary reverence. Magic was still a blessing—but it was also a hunger, and Terra was learning the price of feeding it too freely.

But beyond the Weave loomed something darker. The **Void** whispered in the empty corners of Terra. There were places where magic failed, where the air was brittle and dead, where things with too many eyes and no mouths drifted in and out of being. The first survivors of such encounters returned broken, claiming they had heard voices in the silence, promises of power if only they would surrender.

> [!quote|mark]  Mad writings of Selric the Starved
> _“The Weave hums. The Void listens. One day, it will speak back.”_  

This, too, shaped mortal faith. Some turned back to the Elder Gods in desperation. Others clung to the Ideal Six, believing their imprisonment must one day be undone. Still others raised temples to the Keepers, swearing by the Riddles, the Birth, or the Deep. And in the quiet places, whispers of the Void gained their own worshippers.

It was an age of **divine caution and mortal boldness**. The gods held back, fearing their touch might break the world again. But mortals pressed forward, crafting new peoples, testing the limits of magic, and shaping their own destinies. The balance of power had shifted. For the first time since creation, Terra’s fate no longer rested in the hands of gods alone.

---
In time, what began as scattered hearths became villages, and what began as villages grew into cities. Roads were carved across plains, ships traced coastlines, and the tongues of mortals multiplied into dialects and trade. The scars of the Cataclysm remained etched into mountain and sea, yet within those scars mortals built something new. The First Civilizations had been reborn — fragile, imperfect, but alive.

> [!quote|mark]  Prologue to the Codex of Dawn <br> Written three centuries after the Cataclysm
>*“The gods left us a wounded world. We answered with walls, with towers, with songs. We answered with cities.”* 

Thus began the next age: not one of mourning, but of ambition.
