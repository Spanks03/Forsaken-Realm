## The Age of Cities

The Cataclysm had stripped Terra to its bones, but from those bones rose the first true nations. What began as scattered hamlets among ruins grew into fortresses of stone, cities of timber and marble, and marketplaces where voices of a dozen races mingled. The Elder Gods slept, the Ideal Gods were divided, but mortals pressed onward.

The Age of Cities was a time of ambition — and uncertainty. No single power dominated, for Terra was vast beyond comprehension, and peoples rose at different speeds. In the north, dwarves carved deep into the Spine, reforging steel and recalling techniques thought lost. Across the open plains, elves gathered their kin, building shining cities whose spires reached for the stars. Tritons resurfaced, binding together vast shoals and coral strongholds beneath the waves.

> [!quote|mark] Fragment from the Songs of the Harmonians <br> Recovered in Eriwall Archives.
>  _“It was as though the world itself remembered how to breathe again, and so we breathed with it.”_

Sous, the militant-fortress city, gained renown for its militia, a standing army unmatched in discipline. Its banners, red upon silver, became synonymous with order and safety, and caravans sought their protection. Farther west, the Eriwall Council formed, a conclave of academics who believed knowledge was the only true defense against ruin. Their libraries, built in concentric rings of stone, became sanctuaries where scholars mapped the lingering scars of the Cataclysm.

Not all nations grew at the same pace. Some clung to the old ways, crafting with bone and bronze while their neighbors learned steel anew. This uneven growth created tensions, for jealousy festered where trade did not reach. Still, it was not open war but rivalry, a competition of who could rise faster, taller, prouder.

Religion too shifted in this time. Villages often tied themselves to a single Keeper — the dragon gods who embodied domains of life, like the Keeper of the Hunt or the Keeper of Hospitality. In Sous, the militia carried charms of Datarr, god of Suffering, believing hardship gave strength. The elves of Tira-Valen sang hymns to Yearn, Desire made divine, for they longed always for beauty and greatness. And in the shadows, cults whispered prayers to the imprisoned Ideal Gods, hoping to stir them awake.

> [!quote|mark] Archivist Telen Marr <br> The Folly of Forgotten Faiths
> _“They worship not the gods who hear, but those who dream. What madness drives a mortal to kneel at chains?”_ 

Arcane Radiance became the defining mystery of the era. What had been seen only as survival in the Age of Silence now became the subject of study. Eriwall scholars measured the Soul Fibers of volunteers, charting how magic warped and stretched them. They coined the term _Arcane Trauma_ to describe the tears left behind, and warned that unchecked use of healing spells was no blessing, but an addiction.

Some treated these warnings as fearmongering. In taverns, mercenaries laughed about the “mage’s ache,” boasting of Radiance scars as though they were medals. Others took them seriously, founding the first colleges of regulated spellcraft. These colleges became magnets for young talent, where the weave of the world was debated, measured, and feared.

Sous grew ever stronger, fielding its militia to guard borders and secure trade. Eriwall grew ever wiser, crafting scrolls and diagrams that mapped the ley lines of Terra. Other cities rose too: Marrowdeep, a coastal port that fed half the region; Veythar, a jungle city ruled by Leonin courts; and the drifting city of Solthune, built upon great barges and floating gardens.

But ambition always stirs shadow. As cities flourished, rivalries deepened. Elves sneered at dwarven pragmatism. Dwarves mocked elves for chasing illusions of grandeur. Tritons dismissed the land-dwellers as fleeting, while humans, ever restless, pressed to expand into every corner.

> [!quote|mark] Elder Serrath of Solthune <br>Chronicles of the Blooming Age
> _“We were not yet enemies, but neighbors with too much pride.”_

And then came Deep Oak.

Deep Oak was a frontier city, half-carved into living trees, half built upon their roots. It was known less for its power than for its beauty, a city of green canopies and open courtyards where cultures mingled freely. But it lay near a scar of the Cataclysm — a place where the Weave had always been thin, where magic faltered in strange ways.

One dawn, the air trembled. Witnesses spoke of a sound like fabric tearing across the heavens. A rift opened, unseen but felt, and from it poured creatures no scholar had imagined: beasts of living blackness, their forms shifting as though half-real, their touch unraveling spells as easily as silk.

> [!quote|mark] Survivor of Deep Oak <br> Name Lost
> _“I watched my brother cast fire into its maw, and the fire went out like breath on glass. Then the beast swallowed him whole, and there was nothing left to bury.”_ 

The militia of Sous marched to aid, but even they faltered. Eriwall scholars sent wards and protections, only to see them devoured. Elves loosed arrows, dwarves swung hammers, tritons sang songs of warding — all failed. For the first time since the Cataclysm, Terra faced an enemy none could meet.

The Battle of Deep Oak became slaughter. Half the city was lost, its roots charred and hollow, its canopies torn into ash. Thousands died, their soul-fibers flaring into the sky to repair the Weave or to join the Halo. The beasts, insatiable, spread outward, until at last the combined forces of nations pushed them back, sealing the rift with desperate, untested rites.

> [!quote|mark] Captain Helra of Sous <br> Letters to a Widow
> _“They say the Halo glowed brighter that night, swollen with the souls of the fallen. I did not look. I could not bear to.”_

From tragedy came unity. Nations that had once competed now swore oaths of alliance. Scholars turned their studies from petty rivalries to the pressing question of the Void. Soldiers who once guarded trade routes now trained side by side, sharing banners and coin. And Deep Oak, though half-destroyed, became sacred ground — the place where Terra remembered its fragility.

> [!quote|mark] Inscription on the Rebuilt Gate of Deep Oak
> _“We thought we built walls to divide us. In truth, we built them to stand side by side.”_ 

Sous answered this call with permanence. What had been only a militia, renowned but local, grew into something far greater. The fortress-city of **Sousey** was founded as their headquarters — a bastion where soldiers were drafted, trained, and held to standards higher than any single nation. It was no longer merely Sous’s army, but Terra’s, sworn to defend against threats mortal and otherworldly alike.

Eriwall too transformed. The Council of academics, having proven their worth in battle with wards and research, expanded their halls into the first and greatest center of learning. The **College of Eriwall** rose in spiraling towers of stone, its vaults holding scrolls, maps, and artifacts salvaged from every corner of the land. Students from distant nations flocked to its doors, eager to master the Weave, to study Arcane Radiance, and to prepare for whatever calamity might one day follow.

Yet it was neither soldiers nor scholars alone who charted the future. In the aftermath of Deep Oak, a wealthy family — their name already lost to history, but their influence undeniable — promised salvation not by sword or spell, but by binding nations together in ways unseen. They became known as the **Council of Light**, and under their patronage rose the first vision of a continent-spanning marvel: **Veriwind**, the Train. Though still but a dream, they swore it would knit together distant realms so no city would stand alone in darkness again.

Thus the Age of Cities closed not only in mourning, but in the laying of foundations. A fortress of arms, a citadel of minds, and a council of visionaries — each born from the fear of the Void, each destined to shape centuries to come. Where once there had been rivalry, there now stood permanence, institutions that outlived kings and carried the weight of Terra’s survival.
