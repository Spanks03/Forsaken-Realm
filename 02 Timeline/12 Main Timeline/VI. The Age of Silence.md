## The Age of Silence

> [!quote|mark] **Book of Wynne, Afterlight 1**  
> _“And the world was hushed, for the voices of gods were gone, and only the memory of thunder remained."_

When the war of gods ended, the world did not rejoice. Terra was quiet — not with peace, but with shock. The Halo of the Fallen burned each morning, casting its shimmering crown across the sky, a reminder of how many souls had been lost. The two moons drifted slowly in their courses, pale sentinels in the night. And below them, the surviving peoples looked upon a world unrecognizable from the one their ancestors had known.

The great cities of stone and brass were rubble. The proud roads that bound empires had split into canyons or vanished beneath waves. Forests smoldered for decades, leaving only blackened bones where once there was life. The survivors of Terra wandered among ruins, gathering food from shattered storehouses or burying their kin beneath cairns of stone.

---

In the first generation after the Cataclysm, grief was all that could be spoken. Elders told their children that the Halo was a shroud of mourning — each shard a soul torn from the body of the world. Entire villages would kneel at dawn, chanting prayers of silence as the ring caught fire with the sun’s light. But as years passed, grief dulled into weary habit, and fewer voices joined the dawn rituals. The Halo became not less sacred, but less bearable to look upon. By the time the first survivors’ grandchildren were grown, only a few wandering priests and mystics still held fast to the mourning rites.

---

The moons, however, became the first lights of hope. The people whispered that Nosduh and Ogul had not abandoned them, but slumbered above, watching over Terra with weary eyes. The larger of the two, glowing red with faint streaks like embers, was called **Ogulspire** — the ember that never died. The smaller, pale and veined with dark canyons, was called **Nosduhmere** — the Elder’s watchful coil.

Ogulspire, being the nearer of the two, waxed and waned in but a week’s time. Its swift cycle made it a clock for mortals: farmers planted by its rising, sailors set forth by its fullness, and hunters measured their seasons by its phases. It became the “living moon,” tied to passion, change, and fire — all the restless qualities of Ogul himself.

Nosduhmere moved more slowly, completing its course in a month. Its distant, deliberate pace gave it the name of the “eternal moon,” the keeper of sacred time. Philosophers and priests looked to its calm glow as a guide for patience, wisdom, and memory — the enduring gifts of Nosduh.

Together, the two moons shaped the rhythms of a broken world. Some cultures kept a dual calendar, counting both the short weeks of Ogul and the long months of Nosduh. Common folk said, _“Live by Ogul’s light, but plan by Nosduh’s.”_ Where the Halo of the Fallen was grief, the moons became balance — one close and burning, the other distant and still, yet both watching over Terra’s silence.

> [!quote|mark] **Song of the Moons, Verse 3**  
> _“When the ring burns, look down in sorrow. When the moons rise, look up in hope.”_

---

It was a harsh age. The forges had grown cold, their secrets lost to fire and collapse. Tools were hammered from scraps of fallen iron. Stone once more became the bedrock of homes. In certain hidden corners, fragments of literature and scrolls survived — buried beneath temple floors, or carried by fleeing scribes. These precious relics allowed a few enclaves to rediscover the craft of steel, the language of trade, and the measures of time. But Terra was vast, and such knowledge spread slowly, carried on the backs of wandering traders or drifting exiles.

To most, life was pared down to survival. Hunters tracked the reborn wilds. Fishers carved rafts from scorched wood. Small communities rose from the ashes, some atop ruins of cities they did not understand, others in untouched corners spared by Cassierrel’s wings.

---

The memory of the Cataclysm did not last long as memory. Those who had seen it with their own eyes spoke of fire and breaking stars, of Cassierrel’s wings blotting out the sun, of gods imprisoned in forms beyond comprehension. But their children did not believe. It was too vast, too cruel, too unlike the world they lived in. And so, memory softened into scripture. Sermons spoke of the War in the Heavens. Poets carved the Halo into verse. Storytellers painted Cassierrel as a saint beyond death.

> [!quote|mark] **Book of Wynne, Afterlight 7**  
> _“And the children said, surely these things are myth, for if they were true, none could still walk the earth. And so truth wore the mask of story.”_

---

The loss of the Ideal Gods was felt in subtle ways. Champions once blessed with divine favor now stood empty-handed. Priests discovered that their prayers echoed into silence. Mortals began to shape their own rituals, drawing upon scraps of the Weave that lingered in Terra, but without the gods’ guiding hand, these rites were fractured, local, incomplete. Magic became something feared and revered, a reminder of the powers that had nearly destroyed the world.

Yet among the silence, a fragile spark of hope endured. Farmers discovered new fertility in the ash-thickened soil. Rivers carved new courses, feeding valleys where once there had been deserts. In places where Cassierrel’s wings had shielded the land, fields bloomed wildly, as though nourished by her final breath. Mortals began to speak not only of loss, but of survival — that even in the face of divine ruin, life had endured.

---

Communities swore new oaths, not to distant gods but to one another. “Never again,” they vowed, “shall pride break the sky.” Clans forged pacts of kinship. Tribes shared harvests instead of warring for them. Across Terra’s vastness, the seeds of new cultures stirred.

But they were fragile. Famine still claimed many. Disease spread among weakened populations. Monsters, remnants of divine rage, prowled the broken lands. Even so, every dawn brought the Halo of the Fallen across the sky — not only as a wound, but as a reminder: the dead had bought this moment, and the living must not waste it.

---

> [!quote|mark] **Book of Wynne, Afterlight 12**  
> _“In the silence, there was sorrow. In the sorrow, remembrance. In remembrance, the first spark of tomorrow.”_

The Age of Silence lasted little more than a century, but it shaped the soul of every people upon Terra. It was a time when gods were absent, myths were born, and mortals learned that survival itself was sacred. When the silence finally broke, and voices rose to claim new power, none would forget that before there was greatness, there had been grief — and before there was rebuilding, there had been silence.
