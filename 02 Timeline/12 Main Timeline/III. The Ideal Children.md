## The Birth of the Ideal Gods

> [!quote|mark] Book of Wynne, Verse XXII
> _“And the Elder Gods, seeing Terra full with sky and sea and stone, spoke unto the Weave, saying: ‘Let there be minds to remember.’”_  

The Elder Gods, Nosduh and Ogul, had shaped Terra until it teemed with life. The mountains stood as memory, the oceans carried song, and the skies bore watchful wings. Yet still, the Elder Gods felt a hollowness — for none of Terra’s children knew the hands that had made them. None could reflect the struggles, victories, and truths that had lifted the Elder Gods from mortality into divinity.

So they resolved to fashion children of their own essence — beings of godhood who could walk the lands, speak to mortals, and anchor Terra’s history in worship and remembrance. But even this holy work could not escape the rivalry between brothers.

---

### Eaos, God of Logic

Nosduh began the cycle. From the stillness between stars and the measured breath of tides, he wove **Eaos**, god of Logic. Eaos was clarity given form, a mind untouched by impulse, a voice that cut like a chisel through stone. To him Nosduh gave the **Genasi of Fire, Water, Earth, and Air**, embodiments of elemental harmony, the **Dwarves**, steady and steadfast in their craft, the **Elves**, timeless seekers of knowledge, and the **Loxodon**, patient and enduring as mountains.

Eaos’ first acts on Terra were to raise towers of thought — not of stone alone, but of purpose. The Genasi crafted harmonious enclaves where each element balanced the others. The Dwarves carved deep halls that echoed with the sound of measured labor. The Elves wove libraries in living trees, and the Loxodon carried wisdom across the continents, never hurried, never late.

Nosduh looked on his firstborn with pride, declaring that Terra now had a foundation that could not be swayed. But already, Ogul’s eyes narrowed, for Logic could strip the mystery from the world, and in its place, claim dominion over truth itself.
 
> [!quote|mark]  Book of Wynne, Verse XXIII
> _“By measure and mind, the foundation was laid, and from it rose the towers of reason.”_ 

---

### Truth, God of Truth

Ogul’s answer was swift — **Truth**, whose gaze pierced through shadow and silence alike. Truth was not comfort; he was the fire that burned away the false, leaving only what could withstand its light. From him came the **Tortle**, contemplative travelers who carried their homes with them, the **Tabaxi**, restless hunters of hidden knowledge, the **Firbolg**, stewards of nature’s quiet honesty, and the **Kalashtar**, dream-bound to visions beyond mortal sight.

Truth’s first decree was that nothing be hidden from those who sought. Tortle philosophers roamed the coasts, etching wisdom on shells to be found by strangers. Tabaxi leapt from city to city, trading secrets like gold. Firbolg tended groves where liars dared not tread. The Kalashtar wandered between dreams, sharing visions that broke kings and healed beggars.

Yet Truth’s light could sear as much as it illuminated. Lovers parted when secrets were revealed. Rulers fell when hidden crimes surfaced. Nosduh saw this and accused Ogul of creating a god whose light blinded as much as it guided. Ogul only smiled and asked if Logic was not itself a weapon.

> [!quote|mark] Book of Wynne, Verse XXIV
> _“The light that blinds is still the light that reveals.”_  

---

### Qhathos, God of Glory

Nosduh’s next creation was **Qhathos**, god of Glory, who strode forth like dawn over the high peaks. Where Eaos built minds and Truth bared souls, Qhathos lifted hearts, calling all to reach beyond themselves. He brought forth the **Goliath**, strong as the mountains they roamed, the **Leonin**, noble and fierce as lions in the sun, the **Locathah**, swift warriors of the seas, and **Humans**, restless in ambition and endless in adaptability.

Qhathos filled Terra with festivals of might. The Goliath held trials upon ice-crusted peaks. Leonin built arenas where contests were fought not for death, but for the honor of the fight. Locathah speared the greatest catches from the deeps in competitions of skill, while Humans built cities whose towers were monuments to their own daring.

Ogul saw the swelling pride in these people and warned his brother that glory could as easily lead to ruin as to greatness. Nosduh replied that pride was a flame — dangerous, but the only light some would ever see.

> [!quote|mark]  Book of Wynne, Verse XXVI
> _“Raise your banners high, that the world may know your name, for even in falling, the tale endures.”_  

---

### Temp, God of Rage

Ogul’s counter was the storm — **Temp**, god of Rage. His voice was thunder, his touch lightning. Rage, in Temp’s hand, was not chaos but a sharpened force — the power to rise against oppression, to break the bonds of stagnation. From his storms came the **Minotaur**, horned and resolute, the **Orc**, fierce in battle and loyalty, and the **Centaur**, swift and proud beneath open skies.

When Temp’s followers marched, the ground shook. Minotaur chieftains led charges against any who would cage them. Orc clans forged unbreakable bonds of kinship, vowing blood for blood. Centaur tribes roamed the plains, their hooves striking rhythms older than speech. His temples were not buildings but battlefields, where victory was prayer.

Nosduh called Temp’s fury reckless, warning that rage could destroy more than it freed. Ogul laughed, asking if chains were not destruction in a slower form.

> [!quote|mark]  Book of Wynne, Verse XXVII
> _“Better the storm than the stagnant air, for even in ruin, there is movement.”_  

---

### Vimnera, Goddess of Revenge

Nosduh’s final creation was **Vimnera**, goddess of Revenge, her voice a whisper colder than winter. Rage struck openly; revenge struck only when the heart had stopped expecting. She birthed the **Yuan-Ti**, serpents of ambition, the **Lizardfolk**, patient survivors, the **Goblin**, quick as shadow, the **Hobgoblin**, masterful in strategy, and the **Bugbear**, silent predators of the dark.

Vimnera’s followers learned the art of the long wait. Yuan-Ti plotted for decades before striking. Lizardfolk bided their time until tides turned. Goblins struck deals as easily as they broke them. Hobgoblins built disciplined legions that moved like a single blade, while Bugbears slipped through darkness to settle old debts with quiet finality.

Ogul accused Nosduh of poisoning Terra with distrust. Nosduh replied that revenge was not poison — it was justice with a patient hand.

> [!quote|mark]  Book of Wynne, Verse XXVIII
> _“The arrow loosed in anger may miss; the arrow loosed in patience will find the heart.”_  

---

### Prosper, God of Luck

Ogul ended the cycle with **Prosper**, god of Luck, smiling like a coin mid-spin. To Prosper, fate was a toy, and the line between fortune and disaster a game to be played forever. He brought forth the **Jergal**, keepers of strange bargains, the **Kenku**, cursed mimics of sound and song, the **Harengon**, fleet-footed wanderers, and the **Grung**, jewel-skinned dwellers of hidden swamps.

Prosper’s people lived on chance. A Jergal could vanish with your fortune or deliver a windfall unasked. Kenku traded in stolen voices, Harengon followed whims across continents, and Grung ruled swamp-kingdoms where one leap could bring triumph or death. The god himself wandered Terra, unseen, leaving streaks of strange blessings and stranger calamities.

Nosduh saw him as chaos incarnate. Ogul called him balance — the reminder that no one, not even gods, could control every throw of the dice.


> [!quote|mark] Book of Wynne, Verse XXIX
> _“The coin spins still, and none may say how it will fall.”_ 

---

### The First Age of Divine Influence

Thus the Ideal Gods walked Terra. They spoke to mortals in dreams, in storms, in the hush of groves. They blessed champions, raised temples, and in some cases, took mortal lovers — weaving their blood into the veins of the world. The races flourished under their guidance, each reflecting the hand that had made them.

But beneath the hymns and offerings, the rivalry of their makers lingered. Mortals began to compete as their gods did. Alliances formed not from need, but from whose god they served. The harmony Terra had known in its first days began to fracture — subtly at first, then more plainly with each passing century.

---

### The Elder Gods Step Away

Nosduh and Ogul, watching this pattern unfold, feared that their children might inherit the same ruin that had once cast them from the heavens. They withdrew, each to a distant corner of the Forsaken Realm, leaving the Ideal Gods to guide Terra without their constant shadow. It was not surrender, but a hope — that absence might give the young gods space to grow without their creators’ rivalry shaping every choice.

But even in their retreat, the Elder Gods knew: seeds had been sown, and seeds always grow.

> [!quote|mark]  Book of Wynne, Verse XXX
> _“And the Elder Gods rested — not in peace, but in waiting.”_
