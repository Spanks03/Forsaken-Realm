### The Rise of the Rail Empire

When the Veriwind was first unveiled, the people of Eophoris spoke of it in whispers, as though afraid to anger the gods. A city that could move, a fortress on rails, encircling ports with its colossal bulk before gliding away across the land — it was unlike anything the world had ever seen. At first, it was a marvel. Soon, it became the backbone of an empire.

>[!quote|mark]  Official Record, The Council of Light <br> Year 2 of the Rail
>_"Veriwind shall not merely link cities. It shall bind hearts, unify nations, and carry our people into an age of unbroken prosperity."_

The first seventeen cities were carefully chosen, each fitted into the lattice of the empire’s design. Malmsbury, with its shipyards — both of sea and sky. Los Almohadas, whose deep mines yielded the coal and ores that fed the rail. Bracitos, hammer and anvil of the empire, where weapons and engines alike were forged. Aldlake, lungs of timber, its forests cut down and milled into bones for expansion. Byhaven, breadbasket of the south, where fields stretched wider than rivers. Sousey, the militant heart. Ballybane, its gemstones gleaming in the vaults of the Council. Merrimarsh, fishermen casting nets into brackish waters. Ichinoki, capital of law and politics. Janbridge, where markets bustled day and night. Chang-Chi-Lou, the sin-city of games and coin. Tromsdal, rivers of silk and cloth pouring into caravans.

Each city was tethered to the Veriwind like beads upon a string. At first, this was celebrated. But quickly, reliance became dependence, and dependence became chains.

> [!quote|mark] Propaganda Slogan, painted on station walls  
> _"One Rail, One People, One Future."_

If a city resisted the will of the Council of Light, Veriwind simply did not stop. Without its shipments of food, tools, and luxuries, rebellion quickly turned to famine. It was said that the sight of Veriwind rolling past without slowing was enough to break a city’s spirit.

Four cities were erased from the empire’s map this way. Netherfield, home of ancient shrines, whose priests defied the Council’s decrees. Sage Wynn, seat of seers, destroyed for insisting that prophecy still held weight. Deep Oak, where Void tore open once before, abandoned as “tainted ground.” And Old Cliff, where the waves met high stone and the people refused to bow. Each was not only forsaken, but their names twisted in propaganda, branded as heretical or traitorous so that their loss became a lesson.

>[!quote|mark] Excerpt, Council of Light Decree 47
> _"The forgotten four chose their doom. The Rail does not abandon; the Rail advances. Those who stand against its path are left to history’s silence."_

> [!quote|mark] Survivor’s Account <br> Elder Helyra of Old Cliff  
_"We did not fall to famine. We did not fall to swords. We fell to being forgotten. It is a quieter death, but crueller. I watched our children leave for other cities, their names changed to hide their origins. To live was to forget where you came from."_

The empire’s greatest stain, however, came not from abandoned cities but from its handling of peoples. The Council, guided by radical Sousey officers, declared that the “monstrous races” — orcs, goblins, bugbears, yuan-ti, and countless others — had no place within the Empire’s vision of unity. At first, it was whispers. Then pogroms. Then the Battle of the Continents.

Entire communities were rounded up, loaded into iron-caged railcars, and shipped across the sea to Togruylia. Officially, they were to be “relocated for their safety.” In truth, they were driven into blood sport, forced to fight in great arenas for the amusement of empire citizens.

>[!quote|mark] Propaganda Poster<br>widely distributed in Ichinoki  
>_"The Rail brings peace. The Other brings war. Support relocation, support harmony."_

>[!quote|mark] Survivor’s Account <br> Hruk Stonejaw, orc of Togruylia  
> _"They told us we were being given land of our own. We believed them, because hope is a crueler chain than iron. Then the gates closed, and the only land we saw was sand, and the only gift was a blade in our hands. We fought because to fall was to feed the carrion."_

The Tournaments of Togruylia became the empire’s darkest entertainment. A blood sport, that the captives that bled evolved to be great at the only thing they now knew. Each victory was praised as proof that even “monstrous” creatures could be useful, so long as they served spectacle. For the Council, it was perfect: a pressure valve for empire unrest, and a method of population control for those they feared.

>[!quote|mark] Excerpt, Imperial Broadside from Sousey  
> _"Strength is forged in struggle. The Tournament is not cruelty; it is discipline. Those who triumph bring honor to their kind. Those who fall give meaning to their lives."_

Yet dissent grew. Many citizens whispered that the gods had abandoned Terra because of this cruelty. Others, particularly among scholars in Eriwall, called it blasphemy — not for cruelty, but for attempting to rewrite the purpose of creation. The rail grew fat with goods and gold, but its shadow deepened.

Amid this, Veriwind itself grew into something monstrous. What began as a moving fortress became a city of seventy-five miles, a steel-and-stone serpent that wrapped around ports like a siege engine. Its walls blotted out the horizon, its arcane engines pulsed with stolen soul-fiber, and its banners proclaimed the will of the Council of Light. Wherever it went, law followed. Wherever it did not stop, silence fell.

> [!quote|mark] Academic Treatise, College of Eriwall <br> suppressed text
> _"The Veriwind is not merely transport. It is ritual. Each cycle is a spell cast anew, binding the Weave into submission. Its shadow is not only political, but metaphysical. The Rail itself may be what keeps the gods beyond."_

For common folk, however, Veriwind was both marvel and terror. Farmers prayed it would stop, bringing tools and salt. Merchants feared its absence more than drought. Children painted its crest in chalk, even as their parents wept when it passed them by.

Into this climate rose Tal Honcese, General of the Legion. Radical, uncompromising, he became the spearpoint of the empire’s foreign policy. While the Council wielded propaganda and Veriwind’s shadow, Honcese wielded steel. He looked South East to Osse and declared to claim it for the empire but he personally believed it unfit, polluted by magic and non-humans. His Legion crossed seas, not with treaties but with fire.

>[!quote|mark] Speech, Tal Honcese to the Sousey Legion  
> _"The Rail has cleansed our soil. Now we cleanse beyond. Osse festers with sorcery and mongrel blood. We will march as light upon them, until their shadows are gone."_

Many scholars agree that Honcese’s zeal may have been under the banner of the empire, he was trying to make his own. His campaigns drained coffers, burned countless lives, and drew resistance from unexpected corners. Some soldiers deserted, claiming to see void-spawn among his enemies, as if the void itself had taken sides. Others whispered that his fanaticism was not his own, but inspired by a darker Keeper.

> [!quote|mark] Survivor’s Account, soldier of the Legion <br> Anonymous
> _"We were told we were chosen. But when we landed in Osse, we found not monsters, but families. Still, we burned them. And every night, I saw the ring of souls above, watching. My blade grew heavier with each dawn."_

Despite blood and ash, the empire grew. Cities prospered or perished depending on Veriwind’s mercy. The Council of Light tightened its grip. Propaganda painted the Rail as salvation, while the forgotten whispered of tyranny. The Tournaments echoes of combat carry in the wind. The Legion marched further. And always, the gods remained silent.

## Closing Threads

The rise of the Rail Empire reshaped Terra. It chained seventeen cities into one body, abandoned four into myth, and exiled entire peoples into bloodied sand. Veriwind became not only an engine of stone and steel, but a mythal fortress, its shadow stretching across continents. The Council of Light declared itself the architects of a new age. And though voices of dissent rose — scholars, survivors, rebels — the empire’s grip tightened.

But beneath the propaganda, beneath the roar of the crowds at Togruylia, beneath the thunder of Honcese’s legions, the world itself strained. The Weave, already scarred, pulsed harder against its chains. Void storms flickered at the edges of sky. And still the Ring glowed above Terra, reminder and warning both.
