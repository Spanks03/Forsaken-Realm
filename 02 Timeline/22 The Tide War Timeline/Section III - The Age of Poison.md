## The Leap of Luck

> [!quote|mark] Vaskari saying <Br> inscribed on the reeds of drowned Vask-Tel
> _“When the sea went still, we leapt. When the gods looked away, we landed.”_  

---

## **Echoes After Auraluun**

The fall of Auraluun did not only shatter empires; it rewrote ecosystems.  
The seas that had once glittered with triton light grew heavy with silt and silence. Magic thickened like sap, seeping into rivers and marshlands. From this new ferment rose life that should not have been — quick, adaptive, and unafraid of change.

Where the tritons mourned the drowned order, others heard opportunity in the hush between waves. In the warm lagoons and flooded jungles, the **Grung** awoke — small, jewel-skinned folk who laughed while the world wept.

They claimed to be born of **Prosper**, god of luck and mischief, though the tritons called that blasphemy. Yet Prosper’s mark was undeniable: where others drowned, the Grung flourished.

---

## **The Leap and the Colors**

Grung songs say Prosper dropped seven raindrops from his hand — and each became a different hue of frogkin. Gold, silver, red, blue, green, orange, black. From them came all tribes and fates.

Color is destiny among them, but not imprisonment; every shade believes itself vital to the divine joke. Golds and Silvers guide, Reds guard, Blues craft, Greens hunt, Oranges trade, Blacks dream. Each secretes venom as unique as their voice — some paralyzing, others euphoric, some said to steal memory or summon visions of luck itself.

To outsiders it seems tyranny; to the Grung it is balance.  
The gods roll dice, they say — someone must play each number.

## **First Contact: The Shimmer Wars**

When the first triton scouts sailed into the marsh-rivers of southern Togruylia, they found what they believed to be sickness. The water glowed faintly green; fish floated belly-up, yet frogs thrived in uncountable colors. When the scouts tried to burn the reeds, the fog answered — a haze of spores and songs that blurred memory and turned weapon against wielder.

This was not conquest. It was misunderstanding.

The **Shimmer Wars** were never wars by Grung measure. They were games of territory, of daring leaps and unlucky retreats. Triton forces marched in disciplined ranks; Grung tribes replied with ambushes, traps, and laughter. Battles ended when tides changed or omens soured. Sometimes both sides traded gifts after — poisons for pearls, songs for steel.

Triton records call it “The Age of Poison.” Grung carvings name it “The Great Game.”

---

## **War by Different Rules**

Triton warfare prized order, hierarchy, and oath. Grung conflict was fluid — ritualized chance given flesh. Before each campaign, Grung generals rolled carved bone dice into sacred mud; their pattern dictated who would strike, who would flee, and who would sing. Victory was proof that Prosper smiled; defeat, proof that he still watched.

Their weapons were as strange as their ways: reeds that spat corrosive mist, blow-darts filled with dream poison, war-drums tuned to disrupt spell resonance. Against triton ranks these methods seemed chaos, yet every skirmish followed a rhythm invisible to outsiders.

In time, even triton commanders learned to fear laughter in the fog more than blades in the dark.

---

## **The Pact of Vask-Tel**

After decades of confused hostility, a triton envoy named **Saryen of Tecumheim** dared to meet the Grung chieftain **K’zzura of the Gilded Tongue** at the drowned ziggurat of Vask-Tel. There, amid ruins half-claimed by swamp and tide, they struck a fragile understanding: tritons would leave the marsh channels unmolested; the Grung would share knowledge of antidotes for the spreading sea-blight.

This accord, later called the **Pact of Vask-Tel**, became the first bridge between sea and swamp since Auraluun’s fall. It did not last long — few pacts with Prosper’s children ever do — but for a generation it birthed cooperation, trade, and even mixed expeditions against rogue leviathans.

Triton scribes wrote of the Grung as “the poison that cures.”  
Grung poets answered: _“We are the luck that bites.”_

---

## **The Poison Philosophy**

> [!quote|mark] Silver priest of Nahr-Tuul
> _“To be pure is to be brittle. To be toxic is to live.”_  

To the Grung, poison is not corruption — it is proof of adaptation. They believe that every being must carry its own antidote or perish. Their spiritual code, the **Cycle of Venom**, teaches that creation and decay are one breath; even gods bleed fortune and misfortune in equal drops.

This creed made them unafraid of failure. They did not seek dominion over the seas, only participation — a gambler’s seat at the table of fate. Tritons mistook their unpredictability for malice, never seeing that for the Grung, war itself was a prayer.

---

## **Legacy of the Age of Poison**

Though the Shimmer Wars ended without a victor, they changed the tides. Triton scholars began experimenting with controlled toxins, birthing a new discipline of **alchemical hydromancy**. Grung tribes learned seafaring from captured triton ships, venturing farther from their marshes than ever before.

By the time the **Tide War** truly ignited centuries later, Grung mercenaries fought on both sides — sometimes in the same week. They were chaos given purpose, the unpredictable heartbeat in every strategy.

Today, divers still find idols of Prosper half-buried near drowned Auraluun, smiling through barnacles. Around them grow reeds that bleed gold when cut — remnants of a time when luck itself took form, leapt into the water, and never quite surfaced again.
