> [!quote|mark] Fragment of the Molting-Tablets, Oshin-Kara Hive  
> _“Life is brief. Memory is long. The body cracks; the brood endures.”_

No people in Togruylia are as feared, as misunderstood, or as ancient in instinct as the Hive-born—those whom scholars call **Insectoid**, though their own tongues hold no single word for their collective kind. They claim descent not from gods nor dragons nor titans, but from a principle older than civilization itself: **the Law of Continuance**, the certainty that life must advance, devour, molt, and rise anew or perish entirely.

Their origins lie buried beneath layers of myth, half-remembered brood-songs, and the cracked chitin of ancestors long consumed by their own children. What little we know comes from scavenged fragments, translated pheromone-scripts, and the testimonies of the rare few who have survived captivity among them. The story that emerges is brutal, alien, and strangely beautiful.

---

## **The First Molting: A Myth of Birth Without Gods**

Insectoid myths begin not with a shaping hand but with an ending—  
a great famine in the early ages of Terra, when even dragons starved and the first forests withered into dust. According to the **Brood-Litany of Vha’Skerr**, a ravenous swarm crawled from deep strata beneath Togruylia, creatures driven only by hunger and instinct. Yet among that primordial swarm, one larva molted differently.

> [!quote|mark] The Brood-Litany  
> _“In the dark she split her skin.  
> In the dark she learned the path.  
> One became two.  
> Two became purpose.”_

The myth names her **Skerrith the First Molting**, the proto-matriarch.  
Where others consumed blindly, she began to **plan**.  
Where others laid eggs among corpses, she **built** chambers of safety.  
Where others scattered, she **organized**.

The Hive claims no god granted this change.  
It is their pride: evolution by necessity, not blessing.

Scholars debate whether Skerrith existed, but the Insectoid treat her not as a goddess, but as **the first being who understood pattern**—the first mind to see that survival could be shaped.

---

## **Rise of the Brood-Philosophy**

From Skerrith arose the central doctrine of all Hive-born cultures:

**“The body dies. The brood continues.”**

Insectoid do not mourn their own dead or exalt individual achievement. Rather, they venerate the **chain of molt-memory**, the passing of instinct, knowledge, and reflex from parent to larva. It is believed that each life adds a thin layer to the brood’s collective memory—though never in words or concepts, but as instinctive patterns.

To an Insectoid, glory means nothing if it does not advance the brood.  
Identity is soft; purpose is hard.

This philosophy baffled early explorers. One infamous journal from the expedition of Sir Arden Fell records:

> [!quote|mark] Journal of Sir Arden Fell  
> _“They walked past their dying as if they were stepping over fallen twigs. Only when I stepped on a dead warrior’s arm did they react—  
> not in grief, but in anger that I would disrupt what they called  
> ‘the returning of material.’”_

## **Brood Structure: The Triad of Forms**

Though outsiders tend to lump all Insectoid together, they recognize three sacred life-paths, each arising from different developmental mutations.

None are “subraces” in a humanoid sense; to the Insectoid, they are **evolutionary destinies**.

---

### **The Gregaria — The Endless Marchers**

> [!quote|mark] Gregaria March-chant  
> _“Forward is truth.  
> Hunger is the law.  
> Step and die;  
> step and live.”_

The Gregaria are the most numerous, the backbone of all hives. Modeled after locust-swarms, they embody the philosophy of **movement as survival**. Distinguishable by elongated limbs and twitching mandibles, they are born with a biological urgency that scholars call “kinetic instinct.”

They move quickly, molt quickly, die quickly.

Yet they are revered.

For the Gregaria are considered the **lifeblood** of the hive, the ones whose constant births and deaths allow memory to stack across generations.

Their brood-chambers hum with pheromonal hymns, and many hives believe that in the final breath of a Gregaria, the brood gains its sharpest instincts.

---

### **The Zelus — The Quiet Murderers**

The Zelus are rare, feared even by other Insectoid, for they embody what one scholar called **“the cruelty of efficiency.”**

Assassin-bug in their morphology, they stalk the edges of hives, acting as hunters, infiltrators, and silent purgers of weakness. Their neural venom, which bypasses pain and thought, has led to grim tales among adventurers.

Captured pheromone-script from a Zelus “nursery” reads:

> [!quote|mark] Zelus Pheromone-Script  
> _“Stillness is survival.  
> Pain wastes time.  
> Quiet the world.  
> Let the brood feed.”_

To the Zelus, emotion is a flaw. Silence is clarity. Purpose is absolute.

They are not leaders.  
They are not common soldiers.  
They are **cataclysm** in small numbers.

And their reproductive cycle—injecting live hosts—is viewed by the Insectoid not as monstrous, but as “returning wandering flesh to utility.”

---

### **The Anisoptera — The Sky-Born Matrons**

Among the brood, one mutation is legendary: the rare birth of an Anisoptera.

Dragonfly-like, towering, and possessed of shimmering wings, an Anisoptera represents an evolutionary leap of entire swarms. They live longer, molt fewer times, and think in patterns broader than the simple predator-prey dynamic of other Insectoid.

Nearly all records indicate that Anisoptera act as **strategic overseers**, rarely intervening directly unless a hive’s survival is at stake.

> [!quote|mark] The High-Sky Hymn  
> _“Above the brood,  
> the wind-mothers watch.  
> Above the death,  
> the pattern stands.”_

Where Gregaria rush and Zelus stalk, Anisoptera **anticipate**.  
They design hives.  
They direct migrations.  
They choose which broods live or die.

And they are utterly without sentiment.

---

_Companions, war-beasts, and living tools of the Hive_

> [!quote|mark] Broodkeeper’s axiom  
> _“The brood is not one body.  
> The brood is all bodies.”_

To outsiders, the Hive appears to be composed only of its sentient castes—Gregaria, Zelus, and the rare Anisoptera matron. But among Insectoid themselves, these “thinking forms” are merely the visible portion of a vast and intricate ecosystem.  
The Hive cultivates, breeds, and molds innumerable other species—some birthed directly from brood cycles, others domesticated from the wilds of Togruylia—each filling a precise role.

These beings are not pets. Not slaves.  
They are **continuance made flesh**, extensions of hive-logic, each designed for a narrow but essential purpose in the great machinery of survival.

Those listed below are the most common—and the most feared.

---

## **Iomachus — The Dismantled Cattle**

Among the Hive, no creature is so ubiquitous or so pitied by scholars as the **Iomachus**, a massive cricket-beast bred entirely for harvesting. Cow-sized, docile, and possessing an unusually nutrient-dense exoskeleton, the Iomachus is considered the cornerstone of most hives’ material economy.

> [!quote|mark] Gregaria butchering chant  
> _“Crack the shell.  
> Bleed the pulp.  
> Use the all.  
> Waste is death.”_

Every part of the Iomachus is used.  
Its chitin becomes armor and shields.  
Its tendon-filaments become bowstrings.  
Its meat feeds larvae.  
Its hollowed thorax becomes a brood-urn.

The Hive treats Iomachus with no cruelty—only total utilitarianism. Their existence is not mourned nor celebrated. They are the hive’s raw matter.

Insectoid sometimes use the word **“Iomachus”** as an insult for humanoids who serve no strategic purpose.

And one final warning whispered among explorers:

**“Where the crickets are, the Hive is near.”**

---

## **Kysolampis — The War-Fire Lanterns**

If the Iomachus sustains the Hive, the **Kysolampis** defends it with terrifying spectacle. These melon-sized fireflies emit a cold blue bioluminescence, but only ignite into full brilliance when stirred by a specific wing-rubbing frequency.

This sound—called the **War Call**—is unmistakable.

> [!quote|mark] Survivor’s account, Battle of Thress Hollow  
> _“One glow became a hundred.  
> A hundred became a sun.  
> Then the sun burst.”_

When a Kysolampis dies while lit, its abdomen ruptures in a blast of volatile chemical fire. The Hive weaponizes them ruthlessly:

- swarms are guided above enemy lines
    
- Zelus infiltrators trigger the War Call
    
- the sky erupts in incandescent blue flame
    

Worse: Insectoid storm-priests discovered that if a Kysolampis is struck with a small arc of lightning at the moment of ignition, its death-flash projects forward in a narrow beam—**a lightning lance carved from burning light**.

Armies call them “Sky Mines.”  
The Hive calls them “useful.”

---

## **Nemotocera — The Swarm That Sleeps**

The **Nemotocera** are gnat-like horrors the size of melons—mindless, ravenous, and birthed in some of the most disturbing reproductive cycles among the Hive. When a Zelus implants its egg inside a living host, the resulting larva grows alongside its siblings, but many fail to develop fully into new Zelus.

Those failures become Nemotocera.

They remain dormant inside corpses—enemy or Insectoid—sometimes for weeks. They require no nourishment while sleeping. Entire battlefields become landmines of hibernating young.

> [!quote|mark] Oath of a Nemotocera shepherd  
> _“Wake them only when the enemy approaches.  
> Let hunger be their guide.”_

When disturbed or summoned by pheromone command, they erupt in clouds so loud that many describe the sound as a distant waterfall. Their flapping wings act as both alarm system and weapon: enemies often hear them coming miles away, but never know from which direction the swarm will strike.

Nemotocera rarely kill outright.  
They **weaken**, **distract**, and **overwhelm**, preparing prey for the Gregaria or Zelus.

---

## **Oryctes — Siege Horns of the Hive**

Where humanoids keep horses, the Insectoid ride **Oryctes**, massive rhinoceros beetles with armor so hard that most weapons cannot pierce it without specialized technique. They are slow, steady, and tireless: perfect mounts for long migrations across Togruylia’s fractured terrain.

But their true terror lies beneath the wing-casing.

Oryctes carry hives within them.

Hidden in the warm, hollow chambers beneath their shell rest **2–24 Nemotocera**, ready to burst forth if the mount is attacked. Some Oryctes even transport larvae of Gregaria or Zelus, making them living cradles as well as war-beasts.

Their horn—shaped like a jagged T—serves as a natural battering ram. In tournament sieges, Oryctes columns form the vanguard, smashing gates and walls with thunderous force while surviving impossible counterattacks.

> [!quote|mark] Arena Judge’s Log  
> _“They did not ride them.  
> They marched beside them,  
> as priests might walk beside a sacred engine.”_

To the Hive, the Oryctes is more than a mount.  
It is a moving fortress, a brood-womb, and a siege weapon in one.

---

## **Tinea — Moths of Dust and Nightmare**

Air superiority among the Hive belongs to the **Tinea**, colossal moths bred not for endurance but for battle. Their wings shed a fine shimmering powder—harmless at first glance, but catastrophic during prolonged exposure.

This dust induces:

- disorientation
    
- exhaustion
    
- insomnia
    
- heightened anxiety
    
- a faint pheromonal “tag” detectable by Insectoid scouts for days
    

The Tinea’s dust does not vanish when it settles.  
Instead, it clings to skin and clothing, turning entire caravans into glowing beacons visible to Hive patrols.

> [!quote|mark] Dust-poison survivor  
> _“I could not sleep.  
> I felt them watching.  
> I kept washing, but the dust stayed.”_

Used primarily for battlefield marking and aerial harassment, Tinea are considered too precious for long travel. They tire easily, but their initial charge is devastating.

A single dive from a Tinea flight can peel apart a formation, coat it in exhaustion dust, and mark survivors for later tracking. In tournament arenas, they are unleashed during night rounds, turning the sky into a shimmering haze of lethal glitter.

---

## **How the Hive Uses These Beings**

Each creature—sentient or mindless—fits into the Hive’s philosophy of Continuance.

Iomachus feed the brood.  
Kysolampis light the battlefield.  
Nemotocera guard the dead and punish intruders.  
Oryctes carry future generations and shatter fortresses.  
Tinea mark prey and weaken armies.

To the Hive, none are lesser.  
None are greater.  
All are parts of the pattern.

> [!quote|mark] Brood-priest litany  
> _“We walk on many legs.  
> We fly on many wings.  
> The brood is one.  
> The brood is all.”_

## **Language of Scent and Click**

Understanding an Insectoid requires abandoning humanoid assumptions about communication. Their primary language is not spoken, but emitted—complex pheromone clouds layered with meaning. Clicked mandible speech exists, but is crude and often incomprehensible to other races.

Several scholars have died attempting translation.

A recovered note from the mage-cryptolinguist Orilo Peth reads:

> [!quote|mark] Final notes of Orilo Peth  
> _“Their words are not words.  
> Their stories are not stories.  
> They speak in hunger and heat.  
> To understand them is to surrender being human.”_

He vanished shortly after writing this.

## **The First Encounter: The Blood Dunes Massacre**

The earliest confirmed encounter between Insectoid and humanoids occurred on the jagged eastern dunes of Togruylia, when a trade caravan intruded upon a brood-lane during a migration.

Not a single traveler survived.

The only record comes from a dying scribe whose fingers were stained in Gregaria ichor:

> [!quote|mark] Scribe’s Testimony  
> _“They did not attack.  
> We walked into their path and they walked through us.  
> Like water, like sand, like hunger given legs.”_

This event cemented the reputation of the Hive as merciless killers—  
but in truth, they had not been killing out of malice.

The brood had simply been **moving forward**, as they must.

---

## **The Hive and the Tournament**

When monstrous races were segregated into Togruylia’s three factions, the Hive were placed within **The Hive**—a name they had already used for themselves long before humanoids adopted it.

In the tournament, the Insectoid excelled not by individual strength but by strategy.

Gregaria swarms overwhelmed enemies by number.  
Zelus assassins crippled champions in the dark.  
Anisoptera manipulated entire battlefields.

Many competitors believed the Hive cheated.  
In truth, they were simply **practicing what they had always been**.

A famous tournament log from Judge Karan Felis reads:

> [!quote|mark] Arena Log 77-B  
> _“I do not understand how one species can be everywhere at once.  
> They strike as a storm of claws.  
> They retreat as a whisper.  
> They do not celebrate victory.  
> They simply molt and begin again.”_

---

## **Death, Molting, and Continuance**

Among the Hive, death is not an ending, but a material transition. Bodies are consumed, recycled, repurposed, or laid within brood-chambers to provide chitin for larval nurseries.

To outsiders, this cannibalism is horrifying.  
To the Insectoid, it is sacred.

A brood-priest of the Oshin-Kara hive once explained to a human scholar:

> [!quote|mark] The Chitin-Priest  
> _“When your dead rot, their purpose ends.  
> When ours are eaten, their purpose continues.  
> Which of us honors our ancestors?”_

This philosophy has led to brutal practices.  
Weakness is culled.  
Larvae that hatch malformed are fed to their siblings.  
Zelus broods select hosts for reproductive use without hesitation.

And yet, beneath the horror lies an undeniable logic:  
**the Hive does not waste.**

## **Songs of the Hive**

Though they do not sing in a humanoid sense, Insectoid produce rhythmic clicking, buzzing, and wing-harmonics used during migrations or war.

These “songs” are hypnotic, unsettling, and rumored to induce panic or awe in other species.

One preserved verse reads:

> [!quote|mark] The Wing-Rhythm of Awakening  
> _“Crack the shell,  
> drink the sun,  
> rise and walk,  
> until walking ends.”_

---

## **Artifacts of the Hive**

Despite lacking metallurgy or writing, the Hive possess artifacts of immense cultural weight.

### **The Mnemoshard**

A crystalline resin sac found in ancient brood-chambers, believed to contain the memory of dozens of generations. When shattered, it releases a cloud of pheromone-history.

### **The Storm-Claw**

A hardened chitin blade wielded by a legendary Anisoptera during the Battle of Seven Feasts. Said to cut stone as easily as flesh.

### **The Final Husk**

An immense exoskeleton believed to belong to Skerrith herself. Zelus broods guard it fanatically; scholars who have seen it report an overwhelming sense of dread.

---

## **Modern Diaspora and Fears**

Though contained mostly to Togruylia, isolated sightings of small Insectoid groups appear in Osse’s marshlands and the deep caverns beneath Eophoris.

These sightings terrify scholars—not because a few individuals are dangerous, but because where one Insectoid appears, a brood is always near.

And a brood becomes a hive.

And a hive becomes a migration.

A common saying in the Underbridge slums goes:

> [!quote|mark] Underbridge proverb  
> _“If you see one bug,  
> the wall is already hollow.”_

---

## **The Hive’s Philosophy on Other Races**

To an Insectoid, other species are not friends or foes.  
They are **resources**, **obstacles**, or **predators**.

Humanoids see this as dehumanizing.  
The Hive sees it as clarity.

A scholar once asked an Anisoptera matron whether the Hive considered humans equal.

Her response:

> [!quote|mark] The Matron’s Answer  
> _“Equal? No.  
> Useful? Often.  
> Dangerous? Always.”_

---

## **Closing Analysis: The Terrible Beauty of Continuance**

The Insectoid are not evil.  
They are not good.  
They are not even neutral in a humanoid sense.

They are **beyond alignment**, existing in a space where instinct meets inherited memory, where evolution acts in generations rather than lifetimes.

Their brutality is not malice.  
Their hierarchy is not tyranny.  
Their philosophy is not cruelty.

It is **Continuance**.

As the final lines of the Brood-Litany state:

> [!quote|mark] The Brood-Litany, Final Verse  
> _“We molt.  
> We rise.  
> We feed.  
> We remember.”_

And so the Hive endures—  
a shifting, living monument to survival,  
forever marching forward  
across the blood-soaked soil of Togruylia  
and, perhaps one day, beyond.
