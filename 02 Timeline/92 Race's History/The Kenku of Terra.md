>[!quote|mark]  Old Kenku proverb_
> _“Our wings are ash, our songs are stolen—but the silence remembers us.”_

The Kenku were not born of wrath or vengeance, but of silence. Once, they were a radiant flock within the Skyborne Choir of the Aarakocra, chosen by **Prosper**, god of fortune and song, to carry melodies across creation. Their voices were said to weave luck itself—songs that could turn famine to feast, war to peace, and sorrow to laughter. But when the Skyborne Choir split after the War of the Falling Skies, the Kenku’s tragedy began.

### **The Fall of the Songbearers**

Long ago, when the Keepers warred among themselves, a splinter of the Aarakocra turned their voices toward the mortal world. They sought to teach humankind the divine songs of Prosper, believing that music could bridge heaven and earth. But one among them, **Korril the Choirbreaker**, coveted the gift of fortune for himself. He led his kin to steal Prosper’s Song of Becoming—a melody said to grant creation itself to those who could sing it in harmony.

When the choir raised their stolen voices, the melody fractured. The sound tore through sky and soul, shattering wings, unmaking song, and scattering the flock to the earth. The Aarakocra called it _The Unmaking Chord_. The cursed became earthbound, voiceless—able only to echo the sounds of others. Their wings blackened, their feathers fell, and their eyes lost the sky’s reflection.

It was **Prosper** who found pity in them. He could not restore what had been stolen, but he gave them mimicry—the faintest shard of what they’d lost.  
_"Let their curse be also their salvation,"_ he said. _"For even a broken echo can teach the world what it has forgotten."_

Thus, the **Kenku** were born.

---

### **Kenku in the Present Age**

Today, the Kenku wander in hushed flocks and hidden cities known as **Ground-Nests**—vast subterranean warrens carved beneath forgotten ruins and forests. Within these cities, every sound is sacred. The clang of a hammer, the whisper of wind, the laughter of children—all are captured, memorized, and replayed in haunting mimicry. To a Kenku, the act of echoing the world is a form of worship—a way to stitch together the song they once destroyed.

Kenku society is divided between two paths:

- **The Harmonists**, who seek redemption through imitation and artistry. They forge instruments, craft perfect replicas, and whisper stolen words to teach others the beauty of sound.
    
- **The Discordant**, who despise their curse. They steal voices not to remember but to silence others—believing that only through taking what was lost can they reclaim their divine gift.
    

In the underworld, Kenku are prized as spies, informants, and forgers. Their ability to replicate handwriting, speech, and even entire performances makes them invaluable to thieves’ guilds and shadow markets. Yet among their own kind, this talent carries divine meaning: to forge is to _recreate_, and in recreation lies hope.

---

### **Cultural Notes**

- **Religion:** While many Kenku whisper Prosper’s name, they do not pray aloud. Their worship is silent, shaped through rhythm, gesture, and music crafted from echo.
    
- **Craftsmanship:** Kenku are master smiths, musicians, and imitators. Every Ground-Nest has its **Echo Hall**, a chamber where the sounds of the outside world are replayed endlessly—a living archive of memory.
    
- **Names:** Their names are not spoken but _performed_—each one a sound unique to them. Among outsiders, they adopt approximations (like “Click,” “Whisper,” “Drum,” or “Echo”).
    

---

### **Regional Variants of the Kenku**

Though united by curse, the Kenku have adapted to their surroundings, their plumage and mannerisms reflecting the lands they inhabit.

- **Ashwings** – Found in the volcanic ranges of Togruylia, these Kenku have soot-dark feathers and molten orange eyes. They dwell among the Horde’s smoldering ruins, serving as scavengers and whispering historians of war. Their voices echo like crackling embers.
    
- **Galecloaks** – Descendants of those who lingered near Aarakocra peaks, with ragged gray plumage and powerful legs built for running across high cliffs. They believe that if they can one day mimic the perfect sound of wind, their wings will return.
    
- **Hollowcallers** – Forest-dwellers with feathers the color of bark and shadow, haunting the Hypnotic Wilds. They can mimic animal calls with eerie accuracy and serve as spirit-guides or illusionists.
    
- **Glassbeaks** – City Kenku, often with smooth, shiny black feathers and quicksilver eyes. They are unrivaled mimics of speech and masters of disguise, their voices a blend of hundreds of others.
    
- **Frostquills** – Inhabitants of the Vontrie Snow Plains and Oceri coastlines, their feathers are pale gray and white, their eyes icy blue. They use mimicry to hunt in blizzards, their false cries luring prey into ambush.

##  _The Language of Echoes_

_A Cultural Addendum on the Kenku of Terra_

When Prosper took pity upon the wingless, voice-shattered remnants of the Aarakocra’s kin, he did not restore their songs—he taught them to _listen._ The Kenku became children of silence and memory, wanderers bound by echo and rhythm. The curse that stole their voices did not make them mute; it made them mirrors.

>[!quote|mark]  Erek’tal <br> Keeper of the Last Chord
> “We do not speak—we remember aloud.”  


### The Nature of the Curse

The Kenku curse, the _Unmaking Chord_, was born from a single act of hubris. In the First Choir of Creation, it is said that a flock of proud singers tried to steal the final verse of the gods’ song—the one that could make mortals divine. When they sang it aloud, their harmony shattered the sky, and their voices were torn from them. Their wings fell as ash. Their song was devoured by silence.

Prosper, moved by their anguish, took them in as _orphans of sound_. Yet even he could not undo a divine wound. Instead, he gave them _memory_—so that though they could no longer create, they would never forget.

---

### Speech of Borrowed Voices

Kenku cannot form original sound. Every word they utter is a fragment of something they’ve heard before—an echo of the world’s noise stitched into meaning.  
To outsiders, their speech is eerie but mesmerizing: sentences formed from the whispers of guards, laughter of children, the crackle of fire. Each Kenku becomes a walking archive of lives they’ve crossed.

>[!quote|mark]   Salla of the Hushed Market
> “Our words are not lies. They are memories. The truest sound a mortal can make.”  

In cities, a Kenku merchant might speak in the deep baritone of a dwarven smith to sound trustworthy, or in a noblewoman’s lilting accent to gain favor. Among their own, every sound carries nuance: tone, timing, even _who_ they’re borrowing from matters more than the literal words.

---

### Kenku Cant — The Rhythm of Silence

Between themselves, Kenku communicate through _Kenku Cant_—a rhythmic language of taps, wingbeats, and throat-clicks. It resembles a percussive morse, spoken through rhythm rather than words.  
A pair of Kenku can pass simple information in total darkness, their dialogue hidden within the beat of rain on stone or the creak of wooden beams.

This rhythm is sacred. It is believed that each tone echoes the heartbeat of the _First Song_—the divine melody that once birthed the skies. Every child learns it in the nesting caverns beneath the ruined spires of their ancestors, where echoes never die.

---

### The Echo Language

In mixed company, Kenku rely on _gesture mimicry_, imitating the expressions, posture, and tone of those they’ve seen before. A tilt of the head, a wave, a perfect recreation of a smile—these serve as syntax in their _Echo Language_. It’s not merely imitation; it’s empathy through reflection.  
To study a Kenku is to see yourself reflected in them—an unnerving and profound experience for those unused to being watched so closely.

---

### Collectors of Sound

Young Kenku are taught that “a voice is a treasure.” To them, sound is sacred currency. They travel the world not for gold, but for _voices_—seeking to hear and memorize the rarest tones, dialects, or songs.  
The most revered wanderers are called _Thousand-Tongues_, elders who can weave entire stories from a hundred borrowed memories without repeating a single sound.

Some carry glass vials called _Whisper Jars_, enchanted to preserve faint sounds—a final laugh, a dying wish, the last note of a melody. The Kenku say that when the gods allow them to sing again, they will pour these jars into the heavens and complete the song they once broke.

---

### Faith and Redemption

Prosper’s influence lingers strongest among the Kenku. They build shrines not from stone but from _echo_: small wind chimes, whispering reeds, or resonant caverns that hum when the wind blows just right. These shrines are silent until the right breeze or footstep passes—then they “speak” the collected fragments of prayer.

Their priests, called _Echo-Keepers_, wander between flocks, gathering sounds like others gather alms. Each echo they preserve is believed to mend a single note of the divine harmony they lost.

>[!quote|mark]  Rellin of the Quiet Choir
> _“We do not ask for wings. Only that the world remember the sound of us.”_  


### **Kenku Philosophy**

To the Kenku, voice is identity. Their mimicry is not mockery but reverence—a way to remember the divine music lost to their ancestors. Every sound stolen, every word repeated, is a fragment of a greater harmony they hope to rebuild.

>[!quote|mark] Lirrek the Forger-Priest
> _“We are the echo of the first note, still waiting for the song to end.”_ 

Yet not all believe redemption is possible. Some, especially among the Discordant, whisper that the Song of Becoming still exists, buried deep in the ruins of the Skyborne Choir. They seek to steal it again—believing that only through repeating their ancestor’s sin can they finally undo it.

---

### **Kenku Legacy**

While the Aarakocra still pity their fallen kin, they fear them as well. To many Skyborne, the Kenku’s curse is a reflection of their own potential for failure. Yet when a Kenku and Aarakocra meet, the world itself seems to hush—for one remembers the sky, and the other still sings of it.

And though their wings are broken, the Kenku have learned a truth the heavens never could:  
that even in silence, _the echo endures._
