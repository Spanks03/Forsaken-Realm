>[!quote|mark] Saying of the Crown Jewel
> _Stone is patient, stone is unyielding. To be a dwarf is to be both._

The dwarves were brought into existence by **Eaos, the God of Logic**, forged not in fire or storm but in thought. Eaos saw in the shaping of stone a metaphor for order, permanence, and patience. To him, dwarves were meant to be the pillars upon which civilization could rest — tireless laborers, shrewd craftsmen, and logical builders of enduring things. From their earliest days, dwarves became known for their strength and endurance. When famine, plague, or flood toppled other peoples, dwarves survived by grit and method.

Yet their place in wider society was often lowly. They became the backbone of the working class — miners, smiths, stonecutters — rarely crowned kings beyond their own holds. The saying still circulates in taverns across Terra.
![[Dwarves.png|right lp|700]]
>[!quote|mark] Saying of the Ekonum Clans
>_“A dwarf builds what others dream. A dwarf endures what others curse. A dwarf remembers what others forget.”_

After the Cataclysm and Cassierrel’s sacrifice, dwarves were among the first to rush to the Spine, the titanic mountains raised from her body. At first their migration was framed as pilgrimage, a return to the bones of the savior-dragon who shielded them. But reverence gave way to rivalry. Mountains became not only holy but political, each peak a throne of stone that could be claimed by strength and lineage. At the center stood **Khazaduum**, the Crown Jewel — to possess it was to be the **Crown and Hammer**, the King of All Kings.

So began the **War of the Dwarven Kings**, a war that dragged on for generations. Siegecraft was elevated to an art: clans tunneled beneath rival halls, dammed rivers to drown valleys, or starved their enemies by cutting trade tunnels. Songs of that era remain grim.

>[!quote|mark] Tome of Tourahuum <br>Elder Dwarf teachings
>_“Stone against stone, hammer against helm, we cracked mountains but not each other’s will.”_

When the war was nearly decided, the lesser **House Duergan** tried to seize Khazaduum in its rivals’ weakness. Their betrayal united the warring clans against them. Defeated, the Duergan were banished into the Mines of Khazaduum, condemned never to see the sun again. Generations later, they became the **Duergar**, the Grey Dwarves of the Underchasm.

The victors codified peace with the **Treaty of Crown and Hammer**, declaring Khazaduum the eternal seat of the King of All Kings, and creating the **Kazak Barel**, the council of mountain-kings who ruled their own peaks but swore fealty to the Crown Jewel. Yet peace was fragile. When one Crown and Hammer was judged weak, the council revolted. This was the **War of the Broken Mountain**. Unlike the slow grinding of the first war, this was short and bloody, lasting little more than a year. Armies clashed on icy ridges, avalanches swallowed legions, and one mountain split open entirely, leaving a scar that still bears the war’s name.

The defeated clans were driven from the mountains, forbidden to climb again. They became the **Ekonum Dwarves**, or Ground Dwarves, while those who remained in the peaks were called **Thrummaz**, the Ascended. The Ekonum forged new traditions: family crests became gem cuts, each style unique to a clan. Wearing the wrong cut could be taken as a blood-feud declaration. Many travelers have paid with their lives for wearing a simple ring cut in the wrong fashion.

Resentment boiled over in the **Siege of the Pawns**, when the Ekonum, with aid from other races, attempted to storm Khazaduum itself. They were crushed within weeks. The Kazak Barel debated their punishment, until House Almohodas proposed sending them to seek the **Relic of Myral**. The losing clans were exiled to the cursed ridges near Las Almohodas, which began as a prison but grew into a coal-mining town. The House of Almohodas itself dwindled over the generations. Idleness and chill claimed them, while their miners endured. A local hymn sums it best: 

>[!quote|mark] Almohodan Miner's Hymn
>_“The coal burns, but the hand is warmer. This is our faith.”_

Over centuries, dwarves scattered and adapted. **Thrummaz Dwarves** of the high Spine grew tall by dwarven measure, with long narrow noses to breathe thin air and auburn or black beards to insulate against frost. Their pale skin bore violet flushes from wind and cold. **Ekonum Dwarves**, broad and stout, thrived in valleys and plains. With keen eyes and steady hands, they became unrivaled gem-cutters. 

>[!quote|mark] Ekonum Proverb
>_“A gem uncut is a truth untold.”_

The **Duergar** of the Underchasm paled to grey, their eyes adjusting to lightless tunnels, their beards whittening or thinning away. Some grew hairless altogether, relying on sharp ears to catch sounds in echoing caverns.

>[!quote|mark] Duergar watchword
>_“The dark is patient, so must we be.”_

The **Almohodan Dwarves** were marked by coal. Their hunched frames, calloused hands, and rasping lungs gave them endurance but scarred voices. Generations of soot left an amber sheen in their eyes. In Merrimarsh, **Marsh Dwarves** adapted to wetlands, their feet broad and skin moss-toned. They wove reeds into their beards.

>[!quote|mark] Marsh Dwarf elder
>_“Reeds bend, stone stands. Be both.”_

By the sea, in Malmsbury’s shipyards, **Sea Dwarves** became long-armed and broad-shouldered, bleaching early from salt spray. They earned the nickname “Saltbeards.” In Bracitos, **Forge Dwarves** took on iron-grey hair and skin stained dark with soot. Their arms bore tattooed runes of flame, marking each forge they had worked.

>[!quote|mark] Bracitos forge-master inscription
>_“My arms are my scripture; my scars are my psalms.”_

Despite these differences, a thread of shared culture endured. The Thrummaz prized ancient lineage. The Ekonum demanded mastery of craft. The Duergar valued silence. The Almohodans worshiped labor. The Marsh dwarves prayed through reeds, the Forge dwarves through flame. All believed themselves children of Eaos, Logic given flesh. As one Thrummaz teaching said:

>[!quote|mark] Thrummaz teaching
> _“We do not pray to Eaos for wisdom. We are his wisdom, walking.”_

In the age of Veriwind, dwarves were indispensable yet under-acknowledged. Bracitos forged the rails. Ballybane’s gems bankrolled empires. Almohodas coal powered engines. Yet few dwarves sat on the Empire’s councils. “The stone is strong, but the crown is gold,” the nobles would sneer. Still, dwarves endured. They built, mined, forged, and remembered.

---
Dwarves did not only have have conflict with themselves but always for the peaks that the tried to claim. To dwarves, the mountains are not mere wilderness—they are sacred legacies. Eaos, their god of endurance and stone, gave each peak as a throne to a family strong enough to endure it. To carve a hall within a mountain is to affirm that gift. Thus, when goliaths roamed into these high places, refusing to kneel to dwarven claims, they were not simply wanderers. They were heretics. Trespassers.

>[!quote|mark] High Archivist Durlin Forgebrand
> _“The gods gave the peaks to those with the courage to claim them. To turn away from a mountain is to spit upon Eaos’ gift.”_ 

### The Stone-Blood Years

What the goliaths call the Stone-Blood Years, dwarves remember as the **Age of Intrusion.** To them, goliaths were wild giants—clans without halls, living as beasts under the open sky. Dwarves defended their mines, their quarries, their sacred halls. The infamous **Battle of Nine Tunnels**, which goliaths curse as betrayal, is celebrated in dwarven sagas as _Eaos’ Judgement_—proof that stone itself can be a weapon against those who reject it.

### The War of Splintered Peaks

When the Stormpath goliaths declared that no dwarf might hold the highest ridges, dwarves viewed it as the height of arrogance. This conflict, which goliaths remember as a duel for freedom, is called by dwarves the **Throne-War of the Spine.** Dwarven chronicles speak of it as a holy defense, a war not of conquest but of guardianship: defending Eaos’ rightful thrones from usurpers.

Even today, dwarven ballads honor heroes who held ridges against towering goliaths—_“stone against storm, and stone endured.”_

### Cultural Consequences

The grudges run deep. Dwarves mutter that goliaths squander Eaos’ gifts by roaming rather than building. To them, a people without roots cannot be trusted. Still, a strange respect lingers. Goliaths are seen as dangerous rivals, yet honest ones, and dwarves grudgingly admire their fortitude.

Rare alliances have forged bonds across the divide. Some dwarves still tell stories of the **Stone-Brothers of Karokhall**, a company of goliaths and dwarves who fought together against a dragon incursion in the northern Spine. Though mistrusted by both kin, they became folk-heroes remembered in contradictory tales: dwarves claiming it was their wisdom that led the victory, goliaths insisting it was their fury.

### Today’s Tensions

In modern Terra, dwarves still guard their peaks, and goliaths still roam the ridges. Each sees the other as stubborn, blind to truth. Yet when greater threats descend—the orcish hordes, the serpents of Togruylia, or the return of dragons—the old enemies often find themselves once again shoulder to shoulder.

>[!quote|mark] proverb of Kazak Barel <br> often recited before dwarves march beside goliath allies
> _“Even a storm will break on stone. But let it rage beside you, and together you may break the world.”_ 

---
Today, the dwarves of Terra are not one people, but many. Some cling to peaks, others sink into the dark, some toil in coal or bog or forge. Yet all share a bond with stone — with endurance, logic, and memory. They fracture, feud, and endure, but in the end, as one old toast declares: _“Stone remembers, even when we forget.”_
