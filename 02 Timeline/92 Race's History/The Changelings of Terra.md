---
The changeling was brought into Terra by **Yearn, goddess of desire**. Where Eaos forged dwarves with logic and stone, and Temp birthed orcs with fire and fury, Yearn breathed into existence something far less certain. She gave her children not strength or permanence, but **potential**, the ability to become what others desired, feared, or trusted.

To the changeling, the world is a stage, and they are both actor and playwright. To outsiders, they are dangerous deceivers. To themselves, they are survivors of a cruel world that never wanted them to be what they truly are.

---

## The Origin of Many Faces

Legends say the first changeling emerged from the reflection in Yearn’s mirror. She looked upon humanity, upon elves, upon every race her siblings had made, and whispered, _“Why not all?”_ The reflection rippled, shifted, and from it stepped the first changeling, neither man nor woman, neither dwarf nor elf, yet capable of being any of them.

>[!quote|mark]  _The Hymn of Yearn_ <br> changeling oral tradition
> _"We are not made of flesh, but of choice. We are what others see — and what we choose to let them see."_  

---

## Early History

In the **centuries after the Cataclysm**, when Terra was fractured and survival was uncertain, changelings were both feared and relied upon. Refugee camps spoke of strangers who shifted faces to steal food—or to save children by passing as their lost parents. No one ever knew which tale to believe.

Unlike elves or dwarves, changelings built no cities. Instead, they threaded themselves into the fabric of other societies. One might live decades as a dwarven smith’s apprentice, another as an elven courtier, a third as a nameless soldier buried under another’s banner.

Because of this, early histories rarely mention them by name, but in every archive there are footnotes: a diplomat whose signature never matched their seal, a commander whose face changed between battles, a thief who escaped through walls that did not exist.

---

## The Faceless Sentinel

One of the earliest known changeling legends is tied to the **Battle of Deep Oak**, when the first tear into the Void spilled monsters into the mortal world. Witnesses recorded that a lone warrior stood at the breach, holding it against the flood long enough for civilians to flee. Yet every survivor described them differently: a dwarf, an elf, a woman with fiery hair, a man in black armor.

Historians argue still. Changelings claim it was one of their own—the first to embrace their gift not as trickery, but as a weapon of salvation. They call this figure _the Faceless Sentinel_, a patron of changeling courage.

---

## Enclaves of the Forgotten

While many changelings lived solitary lives, some formed **enclaves**—hidden gatherings where they could show their true forms without fear. These enclaves were rarely larger than a few dozen, often tucked into abandoned mines, ruined temples, or the alleys of sprawling cities.

Inside, changelings passed stories like inheritance. One would recount the lifetime of a priest, another the years spent as a pirate, a third the memory of raising children under a false name. Young changelings raised in such places might never step into the world as themselves, but they carried centuries of _experience_ in their veins.

>[!quote|mark]  Orveya <br> changeling matron of a lost enclave near Merrimarsh
> _"My face is not my own, but my grandmother’s, and her grandmother’s before her. We are one mask made of many."_

## The Wars of Terra

Changelings were never an army, but they were always present in war.

During the **War of the Dwarven Kings**, they slipped between clan courts, delivering messages and sowing misinformation. Some claim it was changeling spies who revealed House Duergan’s plot to seize Khazaduum—others whisper it was changelings who _devised_ it.

In the **Battle of the Continents**, when monstrous races were driven into Togruylia, changelings escaped notice, wearing human and elven faces to survive. Some, however, were captured and branded as spies—publicly executed to stoke fear of the “shifting menace.”

By the time of the **Empire’s rise**, the Council of Light both denounced and employed them. In propaganda, changelings were condemned as liars. In shadows, they served as spies, messengers, and assassins, their existence denied but their value undeniable.

## The Burden of Desire

To be born a changeling is to inherit **Yearn’s paradox**: the ability to be anything, and the inability to be accepted as yourself.

Some changelings embrace this, becoming courtiers, actors, and diplomats, changing forms as freely as breathing. Others resent it, clinging to a single identity until death, hiding their birthright in shame. Many walk a narrow line, shifting only when necessary—never fully belonging anywhere.

A changeling’s greatest fear is betrayal. Trust, once given, is rarely given twice. Loyalty, once broken, is answered with vengeance as patient as it is merciless.

---

## Variants by Philosophy

Because changelings lack a homeland, their differences are not in bloodlines or geography but in **how they live their gift**.

- **Mask-Bearers** – They take one identity and live it until death. Many are never discovered, remembered in history not as changelings but as humans, elves, or dwarves.
    
- **Many-Faced** – They shift constantly, treating the world as theater. Feared as spies, but often brilliant performers, negotiators, or tricksters.
    
- **Ghosts** – Eternal wanderers, changing form and name at every border. They leave no legacy but whispers.
    
- **Echo-Touched** – Rare changelings twisted by proximity to the Void. Their transformations become unstable, sprouting inhuman features or shadow-born anomalies. They are feared even among their kin.
    
- **Doppelgangers** – The most feared and misunderstood of Yearn’s brood. Where changelings shift between forms, doppelgangers _become_ others in terrifying perfection—voice, memory, even habits. Some say they are changelings who lost themselves in too many masks, forgetting their own truth until they are nothing but mirrors of others. To enemies, they are nightmares; to changelings, they are cautionary tales of what happens when desire devours identity.
    

>[!quote|mark] Changeling proverb
> _"Beware the doppelganger not because it wears your face, but because it wears your soul."_

## Religion and Yearn

Though changelings are scattered, many still whisper prayers to **Yearn, Keeper of Desire**. Temples do not exist; their worship is lived. To indulge in love, ambition, vengeance, or art is itself a devotion.

But they do not forget the cruelty of gods. Many changelings mistrust divinity altogether, seeing themselves as pawns abandoned by both elder and ideal gods.

---

## The Mask-Maker of Janbridge

One of the most famous changelings in history is the **Mask-Maker of Janbridge**, an artisan who crafted enchanted masks that allowed goblins and other outcasts to blend into human society.

The Mask-Maker’s true name is unknown. Some say they were executed by the Sous Militia when their identity was discovered. Others claim they live still, shifting endlessly, every mask they make a fragment of their own face.

---

## Perception Across Terra

- **Humans** admire changelings’ adaptability but distrust their honesty. Many human rulers keep “face-tests” in their courts to catch suspected shapeshifters.
    
- **Elves** see changelings as flawed reflections of their perfection, yet envy their ability to embody beauty without bloodline.
    
- **Dwarves** mistrust them deeply, but respect their cunning when wars demand it.
    
- **Halflings and gnomes** are among the few who welcome them, recognizing shared survival through wit and subtlety.
    
- **Monstrous races**—orcs, goblins, yuan-ti—sometimes see changelings as kindred: outsiders condemned for what they are, not what they do.

## Closing

The changelings are Terra’s quiet survivors. They do not march in great armies or carve mountains into thrones. They slip between the cracks, endure through cunning and patience, and live in the memories of others—sometimes as saviors, sometimes as monsters, often as both.

For some, their gift is a blessing. For others, it is a curse. But for all, it is the truth of Yearn’s children:

**To be anyone is to never truly be yourself.**
