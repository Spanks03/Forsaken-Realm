The halflings are the people of the in-between. Born not from divine will but from the mingling of humans and dwarves in the first age of survival, they are perhaps the only race who can claim to be children of _chance_. Their existence is proof that not all greatness must be planned by gods—sometimes it grows in the cracks between empires, in the hearths of borderlands, in the quiet corners no one thought to watch.

Where dwarves carved into stone and humans conquered open plains, halflings learned to thrive in overlooked valleys, sleepy hills, and small, fertile lands no one else fought over. They were never meant to be rulers of nations or breakers of empires. And yet, in the shadows of history, their small hands left marks far larger than their stature suggested.

---

### Origins and Myths

Unlike other races, halflings debate their own beginnings. Some hold to the common belief—that they are the offspring of dwarves and humans, made without divine spark. Others whisper stories that they were once children overlooked by Eaos and Qhathos, who slipped free of destiny’s chains and into the world by accident.

>[!quote|mark] A common halfling lullaby sings  
> _"The gods looked north, the gods looked south, <br> But never down where we did sprout. <br>We grew like roots between the stones, <br>And made the broken world our home."_

Scholars scoff at these myths, but halflings do not mind. Their origin matters less than their survival.

---

### The Early Borderfolk

The first halflings appeared in border settlements—where human farmlands butted against dwarven mining colonies. Too expressive for dwarves, too grounded for humans, halflings existed in a liminal space. Their settlements were small but sturdy, their crops hardy, their homes welcoming.

When others warred, halflings hid or adapted. Some carried messages through battlefields, others smuggled food under the noses of marching armies. They became the quiet glue between greater peoples, known for their hospitality but remembered, when needed, for their cunning.

---

### A People of Quiet Power

For centuries halflings avoided grand wars and the seats of kings, yet their influence was undeniable. In merchant guilds, their caravans tied nations together. In thieve’s guilds, their networks spanned across empires. In wars, their scouts slipped unseen into enemy camps.

>[!quote|mark] Khiltran Forhen <br> Dwarven historian  
> _"Where halflings go, no empire falls alone. They may not hold the sword, but they often set the table upon which treaties or betrayals are signed."_

### Regional Variants
![[Halflings.png|right lp|600]]
Though halflings are found across Terra, the land has shaped them as much as culture.

- **Hillfolk** – The most “classic” halflings, round-faced and hearty. Their villages sprawl across green hills and river valleys, farming in peace. Hillfolk value kinship and hearth more than ambition, and many embody the idyllic “storybook halfling” image others hold.
    
- **Riverfolk** – Slimmer and darker-skinned, these halflings thrive along rivers and coasts. They are boatbuilders, fishers, and traders, known for their quick tongues and keener instincts for reading people. Riverfolk are also infamous smugglers, able to move goods where empires say they should not.
    
- **Marshfolk** – Adapted to wetlands and swamps, Marshfolk bear darker, earthy skin tones and often decorate themselves with charms, shells, and bone trinkets. Outsiders call them “witch-halflings,” for many Marshfolk embrace folk magic, herbalism, and ancestor veneration.
    
- **Cityfolk** – Found in the great capitals, Cityfolk are more ambitious and worldly. Shorter of temper but sharper of wit, they form powerful merchant dynasties, gambling houses, and courier guilds. Some rise to power behind the scenes, but rarely do they put their names on banners.
    
- **Wanderers** – Nomadic halflings who travel Terra in brightly painted caravans or atop beasts of burden. They are entertainers, messengers, spies, and thieves. Often distrusted by settled folk, Wanderers return the suspicion with laughter, knowing no one can ever pin them down.

### A Place in History

Though they avoid the spotlight, halflings have left indelible marks on history.

- During the **War of the Dwarven Kings**, halfling smugglers carried messages and food across warlines, saving countless civilian lives.
    
- In the **Assault of Kninad**, a halfling saboteur named Merrin Trueshade burned the supply lines of the invaders, crippling their campaign.
    
- And in the darkest of tales, a halfling, Errich Boggenlow, was the sole reason that the Realm has information on the Expedition into the Void—a returning journal filled with horrors no other mind could bear.
    

---

### The Vision of the Owls

Among halflings, no legend shines brighter than the **Vision of the Owls**. This warband of halfling warriors tamed and rode giant owls, striking silently from the night sky. They became ghosts of the battlefield—swift, precise, terrifying to behold.

Though few survived into the modern age, the Vision’s stories are still told in halfling villages. A child standing beneath a night sky might look up at a passing owl and whisper, _“The Vision still flies.”_

---

### Hidden Hands

Halflings rarely fight in the open, but their “hidden hand” has changed history more times than most know. Thieves’ guilds in Janbridge trace their roots to halfling families. Several rebellions were fed by halfling smugglers. Even the Council of Light once accused halfling merchants of funding their enemies.

>[!quote|mark] Terv Helmane <br> Human general 
> _"They’ll sell you bread in the morning, wine by noon, and the knife that guts you by nightfall."_

---

### Identity and Prejudice

Unlike half-orcs or tieflings, halflings rarely face outright prejudice. They are too small to threaten, too useful to despise. Instead, halflings face dismissal. Outsiders see them as quaint, harmless, even laughable.

Halflings use this to their advantage. A dismissed halfling is a halfling who can listen unnoticed, slip away unseen, and strike when least expected.

---

### Faith and the Gods

Though not born of a god, halflings worship freely. Some revere Eaos or Qhathos through dwarven or human traditions. Others venerate Cassierrel, the dragon of sacrifice, for saving Terra itself.

But most halflings—whether in hills, rivers, or cities—hold a special reverence for **Prosper, Keeper of Luck**. They see their very existence as proof of his favor: a people born not of divine command but of chance, yet still thriving where stronger races fell. Prosper’s blessings are invoked at every halfling meal, card game, and festival. Dice carved from bone, charms braided into hair, and coins sewn into clothing all serve as tokens of his luck.

>[!quote|mark] Flin Hophaven <br> Famous Halfling gambler  
> _"The gods gave elves eternity, dwarves the mountains, humans ambition. But Prosper gave us luck, and luck is all you need if you play the hand right."_

Among halflings, faith is less rigid devotion and more joyful superstition. Their prayers are songs, their rituals are dances, and their offerings are laughter shared around the table. Yet beneath the lightheartedness lies a true conviction: that life itself is a gamble, and halflings, guided by Prosper, are the best players at the table.

---

### The Halfling Way

Halflings live shorter lives than elves, and less rigidly than dwarves. Their measure of greatness is not in crowns or monuments but in full tables, safe children, and stories worth retelling.

>[!quote|mark] Halfling proverb  
> _"Leave the world a little better than you found it, even if it’s just the taste of supper."_

Yet beneath that humble saying lies a sharper truth: halflings are survivors, manipulators, and quiet builders of legacy. They may not rule nations, but no nation rules long without them.
