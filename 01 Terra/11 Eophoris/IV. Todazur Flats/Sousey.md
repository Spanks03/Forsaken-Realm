### Sousey, The City of Might and Glory 

The times following the Cataclysm were desolate and bleak, especially in the area where Sousey’s great walls and unrivaled armies would one day stand. Many people banded together to survive, driven by necessity, but in the southeast of Eophoris, survival came bound in chains. The Ideal gods had largely retreated from Terra, leaving regions untended; in some, malevolence thrived in their absence. Among Qhathos’ most devoted followers, Viratus was glory in human flesh. He stood a head or two taller than his peers, a mountain of a man whose ego rivaled even his massive frame. Ferocious in combat and eager to earn Qhathos’ favor, Viratus distinguished himself among the faithful. He united his brothers and sisters after the Cataclysm, ostensibly to protect and rebuild, yet his guidance carried a sharp edge: survival in his presence was never gentle. Months after the Cataclysm, Viratus grew restless. Perhaps it was the weight of responsibility, or the silence of his god, but his patience curdled into rage. Failure in his presence was intolerable. A failed hunt meant cut rations; slow labor brought beatings. Any act he deemed counterproductive was met with exhausting toil. And still, his fury mounted. Why should he, the poster child of Qhathos, live in poverty while carrying the burdens of inferiors? Injustice, he believed, demanded redress, and he would become its hand. 

Viratus reformed his people with an iron doctrine: strength alone determines worth. Those he deemed inferior were enslaved; raids on neighbors became the primary method of securing food, tools, and technology. He respected strength, but only that which had proven itself in combat, often against himself. Those who failed, or who could not be molded, were killed or left amidst the ashes of their homes and loved ones. For decades, Viratus’ shadow fell over the land. The people hardened, forced to fight endlessly lest they be deemed expendable. Living only for bloodshed, they became feared by neighbors and despised by those they oppressed. Rebellion was swiftly crushed, violent and absolute, teaching early generations that survival demanded ruthlessness. Yet even under tyranny, the ways of Qhathos endured. Worship persisted, not from benevolence, but because survival under his doctrine was understood as divine favor. In Sous, the silence of Qhathos was interpreted as tacit approval: those who survived and thrived were seen as chosen by his hand. 

After Viratus, many warlords rose in succession, each brutal in their own right. Over generations, Sous’s society crystallized: the strong lead, the weak train, or die. Eventually, the enslaved rose in rebellion, nearly toppling their oppressors. Their victory, though incomplete, was deemed glorious enough to earn freedom. Respect, not mercy, dictated this decision. Opponents of emancipation were purged in subtle or violent ways. From these ashes, the ideology of Sous solidified: merit, strength, and glory above all. As the centuries passed, a fortress city grew and a call to be an Empire's example of it fighting force, with it, the city of Sousey was born. Walls rose high and unbroken, training grounds expanded, and technology was stolen, reverse-engineered, or refined. Sous became a martial state: survival depended on domination of neighbors, at first through raids and later through enforced “protection” paid as tribute. The city evolved into a machine of war, every district, every institution designed to produce combatants ready for relentless conflict. 

The people of Sousey became highly effective soldiers. Children were inducted at a young age, trained in combat, survival, strategy, and obedience. Authority was absolute, discipline swift and brutal. Corpses in the streets were not investigated unless they interfered with training; death was a constant and expected cost of survival. Honor, loyalty, and merit were prized above all. Practicality outweighed laws, custom, and sentiment. Survival was a daily trial. Sousey was intolerant of outsiders. Visitors were watched closely, tolerated but unwelcome, and subject to conscription at the discretion of the Empire or Sousey’s officers. Those who resisted or disrespected Sousey’s order faced harsh consequences. 

A rigid ranked hierarchy defined every interaction. Authority flowed downward, responsibility upward, and each individual understood their place. Duels were mandatory to settle disputes or test skill; refusal could result in shunning, reassignment to penal units, or execution. Challenges could only target those within two ranks above, preserving the hierarchy’s integrity. Promotions were earned through merit, prowess, and survival; failure could be fatal. The military structure grew sophisticated over time. Combat factions formed, specializing in reconnaissance, frontline assault, siege warfare, artillery, or medical support. Large units were established: The Legion, The Vanguard, The Hand, The Beacon, The Lantern's Bastion, and Penal X. Elite units emerged: The Legionnaires, an all-female cohort, clad in deep purple full plate, served as one of Sousey’s Legion special forces. They were broken, reforged, and indoctrinated from childhood, proving themselves repeatedly until only perfect soldiers remained. When one fell, another of identical skill and stature took her place. The Legionnaires executed high-risk missions, clearing fortified positions or striking where ordinary units would perish. The Silent, chosen from both high achievers and expendable failures, were erased of identity, speech, and hesitation. Tongueless and perfectly coordinated, they executed missions that conventional forces could not. Entire fortresses fell without a single alarm, entire towns were erased silently. Among Sousey’s people, they inspired neither envy nor rivalry, only fear. 

The Empire recognized Sousey’s effectiveness and formalized its role. The city became the military engine of the realm: raiding, sieging, and enforcing imperial will in exchange for autonomy, resources, and endless opportunity for glory. The doctrine of the city—strength above all, survival through skill, and the pursuit of glory was codified into law. Penal units were created for the expendable, where failure meant death but survival could earn redemption. Trials by combat adjudicated disputes, biased toward the accuser and designed to reinforce hierarchy and obedience. 

In Sousey, religion and doctrine are inseparable yet distinct. Qhathos is silent; his absence is interpreted as approval of results, not mercy. Survival, combat effectiveness, and contribution to the glory machine are considered proof of divine favor. The god’s worship is both practical and spiritual: devotion reinforces discipline, and discipline ensures survival, which in turn is seen as divine sanction. 

Today, Sousey is a city of lethal efficiency. Every man, woman, and child understands their role, every street is a proving ground, every victory or death a lesson. Peace is a logistical failure, and war is sustenance. The city exists not to flourish, but to endure, and to produce warriors worthy of immortal glory. 

##### Rank 9: Warmaster of Sousey 
Absolute military and civil authority within Sousey Acts as the Empire’s chief military executor Final arbiter of law, doctrine, and deployment Notes: There is only one Warmaster at a time. The position is typically held until death, either in battle or by challenge from a Rank VIII who has proven overwhelming superiority. The Warmaster embodies Sousey’s will. 

##### Rank 8: High Marshal
Oversees entire campaigns, armies, or regions Appoints Marshals and Legion Commanders Can suspend or rewrite local law during wartime Notes: High Marshals are legends in their own right. Challenging them without explicit sanction is considered suicidal and wasteful. 

##### Rank 7: Marshal 
Commands legions or major divisions Arbitrates high-level duels and disputes Oversees recruitment, training doctrine, and logistics Notes: Marshals are feared administrators and battlefield commanders. Their word is law within their jurisdiction. 

##### Rank 6: Legion Commander 
Commands full legions or specialized factions Responsible for discipline, execution of doctrine, and results May authorize lethal training exercises Notes: Legion Commanders are expected to lead from the front when necessary. Cowardice at this level is unforgivable. 

##### Rank 5: Centurion
Commands companies, cohorts, or specialist units Acts as battlefield tactician and disciplinarian Arbitrates lower-rank duels Notes: Centurions are the backbone of Sousey’s command structure. Advancement beyond this rank requires exceptional merit or glory. 

##### Rank 4: Veteran
Experienced soldiers with multiple campaigns survived Often serve as trainers, specialists, or squad leaders Granted minor legal protections and privileges Notes: Veterans are respected, but still expendable. Many die proving they deserve to rise higher. 

##### Rank 3: Proven
Fully inducted citizens and conscripts Expected to fight, train, and obey without hesitation Majority of Sousey’s population falls here Notes: Legionnaires have basic rights only insofar as they remain useful. Advancement is possible, but competition is brutal. 

##### Rank 2: Initiate 
Children taken for training and newly drafted outsiders Subject to extreme conditioning and lethal trials No legal standing beyond usefulness Notes: Initiates die frequently. Survival itself is considered merit. 

##### Rank 1: Chattel 
Prisoners, failed initiates, criminals, or enslaved labor Used for menial labor, dangerous drills, or expendable tasks May earn promotion to Initiate through exceptional action 
*Notes: Chattel are not considered people. They are resources.* 

### Rank & Duels 
A combatant may challenge only within two ranks above or below their own. Refusal to accept a lawful duel results in punishment or expulsion. Higher ranks may refuse challenges deemed a waste of assets. Social Impact Rank determines where you live, what you eat, who may speak to you, and whether your death matters. Advancement brings prestige but also greater scrutiny. Falling in rank is worse than death for many. 
#### The Legionnaires 
The Legionnaires does not recruit. It selects. Composed exclusively of women who have survived trials that kill most who attempt them, the Legionnaires stands apart even within Sousey’s ruthless hierarchy. They are not raised. They are broken, reforged, and perfected. Indoctrination begins early, stripping away name, past, and hesitation until only loyalty and function remain. Clad in full plate wrought to identical measurements and bearing beautiful armaments, unmistakable purple, a Legionnaire is never mistaken for anything else. Individuality is irrelevant. When one falls, another of equal size, skill, and lethality takes her place. The Legionnaires endures, unchanged. They serve as Sousey’s special forces: shock troops, breach-clearers, and executioners deployed where conventional units would falter or fail outright. They advance without hesitation, hold without retreat, and die without complaint. Their coordination is flawless, their discipline absolute. Among the people of Sousey, the Legionnaires is spoken of with a mix of pride and quiet fear. To face them as an enemy is annihilation. To stand beside them is to know that failure will not be tolerated. They are not heroes. They are not symbols. They are proof that Sousey can take even identity itself and sharpen it into a weapon. 
#### The Silent 
The Silent are not trained. They are erased. Chosen from both the highest achievers and the most irredeemable failures, those inducted into the Silent cease to exist as individuals. Names, histories, loyalties, and even voices are stripped away. Upon induction, their tongues are removed. Speech is weakness. Hesitation is death. They move without sound, without signal, and without error. Entire forts have fallen in the night with no alarm raised, no witness left alive. The Silent operate in perfect unity, rumored to share an awareness beyond mortal understanding. Whether this bond is divine, arcane, or born of relentless conditioning is unknown. What is known is that they do not miscommunicate. They are deployed only when annihilation is required. Assassination, decapitation strikes, eradication of threats deemed too dangerous for open war. Where armies would bleed, the Silent simply end the problem. Among Sousey’s forces, they inspire neither admiration nor rivalry. Only unease. Even Warmasters lower their voices when speaking of them. For when the Silent are sent, there will be no battle. Only corpses and a problem solved. 
#### Penal Units 
Penal units are where failure is given one final chance to be useful. Composed of disgraced soldiers, criminals, failed initiates, and those spared execution only because a body was still needed, penal units are deployed where casualties are expected and success is measured in time bought, not lives saved. They are issued inferior equipment, minimal rations, and orders that leave little room for survival. Redemption is not promised, but it is possible. Exceptional performance under impossible conditions may earn a return to the ranks, though few live long enough to prove worthy. Most die unnamed, their deaths logged as expenditure rather than loss. Within Sousey, assignment to a penal unit is considered worse than execution by some. Yet the doctrine remains firm: even the broken can still serve. And if they cannot, they will at least die slowing the enemy. 

#### Laws
The Laws of Sousey Issued Under the Authority of Strength Law in Sousey exists to preserve order, efficiency, and glory. Mercy is not a defense. Intent matters less than outcome. Rank dictates judgment. 

1. Law of Obedience 
All lawful orders given by a superior rank must be obeyed without delay or question. Punishments: Minor failure: Public flogging or forced labor Repeated failure: Demotion or conscription into penal units Willful disobedience: Execution 
2. Law of Rank
No individual may undermine, impersonate, or act beyond their assigned rank. Punishments: Overstepping authority: Demotion or public shaming Impersonation: Branding and permanent labor assignment Repeated offense: Death 
3. Law of Cowardice 
Retreat, refusal to engage, or avoidance of duty without authorization is cowardice. Punishments: First offense: Beating and reassignment to frontline duty Second offense: Public execution or exile Cowardice in battle: Immediate execution 
4. Law of the Duel 
All sanctioned challenges must be answered. Lawful duels resolve disputes. Conditions: Challenges may not exceed two ranks upward Duel severity assigned by higher authority Punishments for Refusal: Loss of rank and privileges Public shunning or expulsion from the city Forced service in lethal training battalions 
5. Law of Weakness 
Repeated failure, incompetence, or liability to unit survival is a punishable offense. Punishments: Probationary training under lethal conditions Demotion Removal as a “weak link” (execution) 
6. Law of Treachery 
Betrayal of Sousey, conspiracy, or collaboration with enemies is unforgivable. Punishments: Immediate execution Public display of remains Erasure of name from records 
7. Law of Glory 
Acts that diminish Sousey’s honor, reputation, or perceived strength are criminal. Examples: Public dissent Refusal to celebrate victory Unauthorized negotiation or surrender Punishments: Forced participation in glory trials Loss of rank Death, if damage is deemed severe 
8. Law of Order 
Uncontrolled violence that disrupts training, logistics, or command is prohibited. Punishments: Mandatory duel to resolve dispute Restitution through forced labor Execution if disruption results in loss of assets 
9. Law of the Visitor 
Outsiders exist at the pleasure of Sousey and are subject to its laws. Punishments: Forced conscription Detainment or expulsion Execution for perceived threat 
10. Law of Conscription 
All able bodies serve. Refusal is desertion. Punishments: Immediate induction into penal units Branding Execution for repeated refusal 
11. Law of Speech 
Speech that undermines authority, discipline, or morale is forbidden. Punishments: Mutilation (tongue-scarring or removal, non-lethal for citizens) Public beating Execution for high-ranking offenders 
12. Law of Survival 
Any law may be suspended if survival or victory demands it. Authority: Rank V and above may invoke suspension Abuse of suspension is punishable by death if failure results Final Doctrine In Sousey, the law does not protect the people. It protects the machine. Those who serve it well rise. Those who slow it are removed. 

#### Trails
Trials by Combat in Sousey “Truth Is Proven by Survival.” Trials by combat are the preferred and most respected method of adjudicating disputes, accusations, and criminal charges in Sousey. Guilt or innocence is irrelevant. Only worth is judged. 

1. Grounds for Trial A Trial by Combat may be invoked for: Accusations of cowardice, incompetence, or insubordination Disputes of honor or rank Alleged violations of Sousey’s laws Challenges to authority deemed potentially legitimate Frivolous trials are discouraged, not prohibited. An accuser who survives is assumed justified. 
2. Right to Accuse Any individual of equal or higher rank may issue an accusation. Accusations from lower ranks are permitted only if supported by a superior sponsor. Anonymous accusations are allowed if delivered through command channels. Bias: Accusations made by higher-ranked individuals are presumed valid unless disproven in combat. 
3. Appointment of the Arbiter An Arbiter of at least one rank higher than both parties is assigned. The Arbiter is chosen from the accuser’s chain of command whenever possible. The Arbiter’s rulings are final and not subject to appeal. Bias: Arbiters are instructed to preserve valuable assets and command stability, favoring established authority. 
4. Determination of Trial Severity The Arbiter determines the form of combat based on: Rank disparity Value of the accused Nature of the accusation Forms Include: First Blood Yield Incapacitation Death Bias: Accusers may petition for increased severity. Accused petitions for reduced severity are rarely granted. 
5. Equipment & Conditions Accusers may select from a range of approved weapons. Accused are issued standard or downgraded equipment. Armor parity is not required. Environmental hazards may be introduced “to simulate battlefield conditions.” Bias: The accuser’s equipment is considered part of their earned privilege. 
6. Representation & Advocacy No advocates are permitted. Character testimony is not allowed. Past service records may be submitted only by the accuser or Arbiter. Bias: Negative records are admissible. Commendations rarely are. 
7. Conduct of the Trial Combat begins on the Arbiter’s signal. Interference is punishable by execution. Yield is only recognized if the Arbiter accepts it. Bias: Delayed yields are often ignored. 
8. Verdict & Consequences Victory by the accuser confirms guilt and results in immediate sentencing. Victory by the accused results in: Formal acknowledgment of competence Mandatory reassignment or transfer Increased scrutiny Bias: Even victorious accused are often reassigned to dangerous duties “to test consistency.” 
9. Refusal to Participate Refusal to submit to a lawful Trial by Combat is an admission of guilt. Punishments Include: Loss of rank Public shunning Expulsion from Sousey Assignment to penal units 
10. Record & Memory Trials are recorded only by outcome, not circumstance. Deaths during trials are logged as “operational losses.” The Arbiter’s judgment is archived as precedent. Closing Doctrine Trials by Combat do not exist to uncover truth. They exist to remove doubt. Those who deserve to live will prove it. Those who do not will justify their removal.
