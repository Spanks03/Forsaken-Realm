You’ll smell **[[Gurn the Smokefather]]** long before you see him.

His stall, tucked along the wind-swept eastern edge of the market, is a haze of **black pepper, hickory smoke, and sizzling fat.** Thick curls of smoke rise from the stone smoker behind him, curling into the sky like offerings to some forgotten god of fire and flavor.

Gurn himself is a **broad, gruff man**, wrapped in an apron so caked with soot and grease it could stand on its own. His arms are like ham hocks, and his beard is streaked white and smoke-yellow. He speaks in **grunts and gestures**, but somehow everyone understands him. Folks call him “Smokefather” out of respect—**not because he’s a parent, but because no one else dares try to smoke meat while he’s alive.**

His offerings range from **peppered boar jerky** and **spiced venison links** to **honey-glazed squirrel tails** (a surprising favorite among locals). All of it is cured and smoked to last **long journeys or hard winters**, and everything he sells carries the unmistakable scent of **campfire and cracked black pepper.** If it doesn’t make your eyes water and your stomach growl, it’s not Gurn’s.

He never haggles. His prices are fair and final.

If you ask him for secrets of the smoke, he’ll just point at the sky and say, _“It’s in the wind. Can’t teach wind.”_

Some whisper that his smokehouse is old dwarven craft, built into the stone with hidden runes. Others say he smokes with woods that don’t grow anymore—**bones of trees that burned in the First War.** Gurn doesn’t confirm anything. He just keeps the fire lit and the smoke rolling.
