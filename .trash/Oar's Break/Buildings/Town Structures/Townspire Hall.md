*Townhall & Post Office*

A slender stone tower rising awkwardly from a cluster of lower timber buildings, **Townspire Hall** leans ever so slightly to the east, as if bowing under the weight of its own archives. Its slate roof is patched in mismatched tiles, and thin iron gutters snake down its flanks like vines. From the outside, it looks more like a forgotten watchtower than the **bureaucratic heart of Oar’s Break**—but the locals know better.

Inside, the air is **dry, dusty, and uncomfortably quiet**, broken only by the rustle of parchment or the precise scratch of a quill. **Walls are lined with pigeonholes, shelves, and scroll tubes**, filled with town records dating back more than a century. Despite the tight space, **every document has its place**—and no one dares disrupt the system, lest they face the calm wrath of **[[Clerk Olwin Resh]]**, town clerk, postmaster, and unofficial steward of memory.

Olwin, pale-eyed and bird-boned, **remembers every letter ever sent through Townspire Hall**. Ask her about a property deed from 20 years ago or a wedding invitation from three winters past, and she’ll likely tell you not only where it’s filed, but what ink was used and how many spelling errors it contained. Locals say she doesn’t sleep, just files.

Beneath the tower’s records chamber is the **postroom**, where brass mail slots open to the outside and a small stable supports a rotating team of hardy couriers. Wax seals are cataloged with obsessive care, and **a strict rule of confidentiality** is posted (and strictly enforced). **No one, not even the mayor, reads another’s mail without a signed court order.**

The uppermost level holds the **Council Nook**, where minor town matters are debated between Mayor Voss and community liaisons. But space is tight, and the scent of ancient parchment so strong, most meetings are short and half-finished before retreating to larger venues.

Despite its cramped design, Townspire Hall holds **births, deaths, trades, fences, fines, complaints, blessings, and curses**—the living record of Oar’s Break. And Olwin guards it all with an **archivist’s pride and a deadpan sense of humor so dry it might crumble if laughed at.**
